    Section 3.5 Applications

    Objectives

    • Be able to apply methods of solutions from previous sections to some elementary problems in applications.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    In this section we will study three elementary examples of applications where the methods from previous sections can be effectively used.
    🔗

    Subsection 3.5.1 Spring-Mass System

    A common example of a second order linear differential equation with constant coefficients arises in modeling the motion of a mass attached to a spring whose other end is fixed to a support. Let us consider a spring suspended vertically from a fixed ceiling (rigid support) with the mass attached to its lower end, as illustrated in Figure 3.5.1.
    🔗
    Let \(\ell\) denote the natural (unstretched) length of the spring, and let \(s\) be the amount by which the spring stretches when the mass \(m\) is attached and allowed to hang in equilibrium under gravity. We assume that the motion of the mass is restricted to the vertical line through the point of the equilibrium of the mass. To take this vertical line (see Figure 3.5.1 (d)) as the coordinate axis, we take the equilibrium point as the origin and assign the vertically downward direction as the positive direction.
    🔗
    Denote by \(y(t)\) the displacement of the mass at time \(t\) from its equilibrium position (the origin). In addition to the restoring force \(F_{\text{s}}(t)\) of the spring and gravitational force \(mg\text{,}\) assume that the mass is acted upon by a damping force \(F_{\text{d}}(t)\) as well as an external force \(F(t).\) See Figure 3.5.1 (c) for wall friction as an example of a damping force. Thus, we have
    \begin{equation*} \text{the net force on the mass} = F_{\text{s}}(t) +mg+ F_{\text{d}}(t) + F(t). \end{equation*}
    According to Newton’s second law, we then have
    \begin{equation} m y''(t) = F_{\text{s}}(t) + mg+ F_{\text{d}}(t) + F(t).\tag{3.5.1} \end{equation}
    \(\bullet\) How do we model the spring force \(F_{\text{s}}(t)?\)
    🔗
    Since a spring that is stretched or compressed tends to return to its natural length, the spring force wants to pull the mass toward its equilibrium position. For an ideal spring—one made of a homogeneous material with uniform coiling—Robert Hooke, a 17th-century English physicist, established that the restoring force required to stretch or compress the spring (without damaging it) is directly proportional to the resulting displacement.
     1 
    For a spring made up of a heterogeneous material and with nonuniform coiling, the restoring force may be modeled by a nonlinear function of the displacement. For example, \(F_{\text{s}}(t) = - k\,\lvert y(t)\rvert^{p-2}y(t)\) with a fixed number \(p\) in \((1, \infty)\) and \(k>0\) a fixed real number.
    This means that
    \begin{equation} F_{\text{s}}(t) = - k\,(s+y(t)),\tag{3.5.2} \end{equation}
    where \(k\) is a fixed positive constant, called the spring constant which measures the stiffness of the spring. The negative \((-)\) means that the restoring force is directed toward the origin.
    🔗
    One may ask: how do we practically calculate \(k\) of a spring? The simplest way to calculate \(k\) is to simply suspend the spring from a support with the mass attached to its other end and observe the stretch \(s\) the spring experiences for its equilibrium under gravity. Since the mass is at rest, the net force acting on the mass is zero, that is,
    \begin{equation*} \text{the net force on the mass}=0 = - ks +mg, \end{equation*}
    which yields
    \begin{equation} mg = ks,\tag{3.5.3} \end{equation}
    and so
    \begin{equation*} k = \frac{mg}{s}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \(\bullet\) How do we model \(F_{\text{d}}(t)?\)
    🔗
    It is intuitive that the damping force (or drag) increases as the speed of the mass increases. Hence, the simplest way to model the damping force is to assume that it is directly proportional to the velocity.
     2 
    For a medium that resists the motion of the mass depending on a power of the speed, the damping force may be modeled by a nonlinear function of the velocity. For example, \(F_{\text{s}}(t) = - b\,\lvert y'(t)\rvert^{q-2}y'(t)\) with a fixed number \(q\) in \((1, \infty)\) and \(b>0\) a fixed real number.
    This means that
    \begin{equation} F_{\text{d}}(t)= - b\, y'(t),\tag{3.5.4} \end{equation}
    where \(b\) is the constant of proportionality, called the damping coefficient of the medium offering the resistance to the motion of the mass.
    🔗
    Using (3.5.2) and (3.5.4) in (3.5.1), we obtain
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m y''(t) = -ks -k y(t) + mg- b y'(t) + F(t), \end{equation*}
    which, in view of (3.5.3), yields
    \begin{equation} my''(t) + by'(t) + k y(t) = F(t).\tag{3.5.5} \end{equation}
    This is a second order linear differential equation with constant coefficient, and it can be solved using the methods from the previous sections. For example, the method of undetermined coefficients or variation of parameters can be used.
    🔗
    We now examine several interesting and special cases of (3.5.5) before discussing the general form.
    🔗

    I. Free Undamped Motion (\(b=0,\;F(t) =0\)).

    When \(b= 0\) (no damping) and \(F(t) = 0\) (no external forces), the mass-spring system is said to have a free undamped motion and the system is called a simple harmonic oscillator. The resulting differential equation for the displacement of the mass from its equilibrium position is
    \begin{equation*} my''(t) + k y(t) = 0, \end{equation*}
    which, with \(w^2 = k/m\text{,}\) becomes
    \begin{equation} y''(t) + \omega^2 y(t) = 0,\tag{3.5.6} \end{equation}
    also referred to as a simple harmonic oscillator. Then the general solution of the simple harmonic oscillator is
    \begin{equation} y(t) = c_1 \cos(\omega t) + c_2 \sin(\omega t),\tag{3.5.7} \end{equation}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants which can be determined when the initial conditions \(y(t_0) = y_0, y'(t_0) = y_1\) are provided.
    🔗
    The displacement \(y(t)\) described by (3.5.7) can interpreted most effectively when it is written in the form of a single sine or cosine function. To express it in the form of a single sine function, we seek to find two constants \(A\) and \(\phi\) so that
    \begin{equation} y(t) = A \sin(\omega t -\phi).\tag{3.5.8} \end{equation}
    Then
    \begin{equation} y(t) = A \sin(\omega t) \cos(\phi) - A\cos(\omega t) \sin (\phi).\tag{3.5.9} \end{equation}
    Since the terms in (3.5.7) and (3.5.9) must match, we obtain
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} A\sin(\phi) = -c_1,\\ A\cos(\phi) = c_2. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Squaring and adding these equations gives
    \begin{equation*} A^2 = c_1^2 + c_2^2. \end{equation*}
    We take
    \begin{equation*} A =\sqrt{c_1^2 + c_2^2}. \end{equation*}
    It is possible that \(A =0\text{,}\) in which case, we have \(y(t) = 0\) for all \(t\text{,}\) the trivial solution, and this means that the mass is at rest in the equilibrium position for all \(t.\) Therefore the only interesting case is when \(A>0.\) If \(c_2 \ne 0,\) then
    \begin{equation*} \arctan(\frac{-c_1}{c_2}) \text{ lies in }\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right). \end{equation*}
    We have
    \begin{equation*} \phi = \begin{cases} \arctan(\frac{-c_1}{c_2}) &\text{if } c_2>0,\\[1em] \arctan(\frac{-c_1}{c_2})+\pi&\text{if } c_2<0,\\ \frac{\pi}{2}&\text{if }c_1<0 \text{ and } c_2=0,\\ -\frac{\pi}{2}&\text{if } c_1>0 \text{ and } c_2=0. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Also, note that (3.5.8) can be written as
    \begin{equation*} y(t) = A \cos(\omega t -\phi -\frac{\pi}{2}). \end{equation*}
    It is now clear from (3.5.8) that the mass oscillates about the origin with the amplitude \(A,\) which is the maximum displacement from the equilibrium position. The time period for one complete oscillation is \(T= 2\pi / \omega,\) and therefore the number \(\omega\) is the circular frequency of the oscillations, that is, the number of cycles per unit \(2\pi\) units of time.
    🔗
    Example 3.5.2.
    A 2-pound mass stretches a spring 6 inches. At time \(t = 0\text{,}\) the mass is released from a position 8 inches below equilibrium with an initial upward velocity of \(\tfrac{4}{3}\) ft/s.
    🔗
    1. Find the equation of motion in both the forms (3.5.7) and (3.5.8).
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Graph the velocity versus the displacement.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Let \(y(t)\) denote in feet the displacement of the mass from the equilibrium position measured positively in the vertically downward direction. We have \(mg = 2~\text{lb}, \text{spring stretch}, s = 6~\text{in} = 0.5~\text{ft}.\) We have \(m = \frac{mg}{g} = \frac{2}{32} = \frac{1}{16}~\text{slug},\) and the spring constant \(k = \frac{mg}{s} = \frac{2}{0.5} = 4~\text{lb/ft}.\) The differential equation of motion is
      \begin{equation*} m y'' + k y = 0, \end{equation*}
      which becomes
      \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{16} y'' + 4y = 0, \end{equation*}
      and therefore
      \begin{equation*} y'' + 64y = 0 \end{equation*}
      with the initial condition \(y(0) = -\tfrac{2}{3}, y'(0) = \tfrac{4}{3}.\) The general solution of the differential equation is
      \begin{equation*} y(t) = c_1 \cos(8t) + c_2 \sin(8t). \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Applying the initial conditions gives
      \begin{equation*} y(0)= -\frac{2}{3} = c_1. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Also, since
      \begin{equation*} y'(t) = -8c_1 \sin(8t) + 8c_2 \cos(8t) \end{equation*}
      and \(y'(0) =\frac{4}{3},\) we obtain \(8c_2 = \frac{4}{3},\) that is, \(c_2 = \frac{1}{6}.\) Hence the equation of the motion is
      \begin{equation*} y(t) = -\frac{2}{3} \cos(8t) + \frac{1}{6} \sin(8t), \end{equation*}
      which is of the form (3.5.7). Using the method discussed for (3.5.8) to find the equation of the motion in the amplitude-phase, we compute
      \begin{equation*} A= \sqrt{c_1^2+c_2^2}=\sqrt{\frac{4}{9}+\frac{1}{36}}= \frac{\sqrt{17}}{6} \end{equation*}
      and
      \begin{equation*} \phi = \arctan(\frac{-c_1}{c_2}) = \arctan(\frac{2/3}{1/6}) =\arctan(4)\approx 1.3258~\text{rad}, \end{equation*}
      and therefore
      \begin{equation*} y(t) = \frac{\sqrt{17}}{6} \sin\!\left(8t - \varphi\right) \approx \frac{\sqrt{17}}{6} \sin\!\left(8t - 1.3258\right). \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Its graph is shown in Figure 3.5.3.
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    2. We find
      \begin{equation*} y'(t) = 8\frac{\sqrt{17}}{6} \cos\!\left(8t - \varphi\right). \end{equation*}
      Then
      \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{y(t)}{\frac{\sqrt{17}}{6} }\right)^2+\left(\frac{y'(t)}{\frac{8\sqrt{17}}{6}}\right)^2 = 1 \end{equation*}
      for all \(t\text{.}\) This takes the form
      \begin{equation*} 272 (y(t))^2 + 17(y'(t))^2= 272 \end{equation*}
      for all \(t\) and is an ellipse in the \(y'y\)-plane as shown in Figure 3.5.4. The graph is called the orbit of the simple harmonic oscillator and visualizes the oscillatory motion.

    II. Free Damped Motion (\(b>0,\;F(t) =0\)).

    When \(b> 0\) (with damping) and \(F(t) = 0\) (no external forces), the mass-spring system is said to have a free damped motion. The resulting differential equation for the motion of the mass is
    \begin{equation*} my''(t)+by'(t) + k y(t) = 0, \end{equation*}
    which, with \(2\beta = \frac{b}{m}\) and \(w^2 = \frac{k}{m}\text{,}\) becomes
    \begin{equation} \label{} y'' + 2\beta y'+ \omega^2 y = 0,\tag{3.5.10} \end{equation}
    and the differential equation (3.5.10) is known as a free damped spring–mass system. Its general solution depends on the relative strengths of the damping and spring forces. When the spring force dominates, the motion is oscillatory with amplitude decaying to zero as \(t \to \infty\) and when the damping force dominates, the motion is non-oscillatory. There exists a threshold value of the damping coefficient—called the critical damping—above which no oscillations occurs, and below which the system exhibits damped oscillations whose amplitude decays to zero as \(t \to \infty\text{.}\) To analyze this, we start with the auxiliary equation for (3.5.10); namely,
    \begin{equation*} r^2 + 2\beta r + \omega ^2 =0, \end{equation*}
    with \(y(t)=e^{rt}\) is a trial solution. It then follows that
    \begin{equation} r = \frac{-2\beta\pm\sqrt{4\beta^2 - 4 \omega^2}}{2} = -\beta\pm\sqrt{\beta^2 - \omega^2}.\tag{3.5.11} \end{equation}
    🔗
    There are three significantly different cases of a free damped motion.
    🔗
    1. Underdamped Motion: When \(\beta <\omega,\) (3.5.11) yields
      \begin{equation*} r = - \beta \pm i \sqrt{\omega^2- \beta^2}, \end{equation*}
      so that the general solution of (3.5.10) is
      \begin{equation*} y(t) = e^{-\beta t}\left(c_1\cos(\sqrt{\omega^2- \beta^2}\; t)+ c_2 \sin(\sqrt{\omega^2- \beta^2}\; t)\right), \end{equation*}
      where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants are determined when the initial conditions \(y(t_0) = y_0, y'(t_0) = y_1\) are provided. The displacement \(y(t)\) is periodic with the period \(2\pi/\sqrt{\omega^2- \beta^2}.\) Moreover, since \(\beta >0,\) we observe that
      \begin{equation*} \lim\limits_{t\to\infty} e^{-\beta t}\cos(\sqrt{\omega^2- \beta^2}\; t)=0 \quad \mbox{and} \quad \lim\limits_{t\to\infty}e^{-\beta t}\sin(\sqrt{\omega^2- \beta^2}\; t)=0. \end{equation*}
      It then follows that
      \begin{equation*} \lim\limits_{t\to\infty} y(t)=0. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      With regard to the velocity of the mass, we find
      \begin{equation*} \begin{split} y'(t) &= -\beta e^{-\beta t}\left(c_1\cos(\sqrt{\omega^2- \beta^2}\; t)+ c_2 \sin(\sqrt{\omega^2- \beta^2}\; t)\right)\\ & + \sqrt{\omega^2- \beta^2}e^{-\beta t}\left(-c_1\sin (\sqrt{\omega^2- \beta^2}\; t)+ c_2 \sin(\sqrt{\omega^2- \beta^2}\; t)\right). \end{split} \end{equation*}
      Again, we observe that
      \begin{equation*} \lim\limits_{t\to\infty} y'(t)=0. \end{equation*}
      Hence, the mass approaches the equilibrium position while oscillating with the circular frequency \(\sqrt{\omega^2- \beta^2}\) and the velocity approaches zero as \(t\) goes to \(\infty.\)
      🔗
      Since \(\beta = b/(2m)\) and \(\omega^2 = k/m,\) \(\beta < \omega\) is equivalent to \(b< 2\sqrt{mk}.\) We conclude that when the damping force is too weak to counteract a strong spring force, the mass undergoes damped oscillations: the amplitude gradually decreases to zero. In this case, the mass-spring system is said to be underdamped.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Overdamped Motion: When \(\beta >\omega,\) (3.5.11) yields \(r = - \beta \pm \sqrt{\beta^2- \omega^2},\) so that the general solution of (3.5.10) is
      \begin{equation*} y(t) = c_1e^{(-\beta + \sqrt{\beta^2- \omega^2})t}+ c_2e^{(-\beta - \sqrt{\beta^2- \omega^2})t}, \end{equation*}
      where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants are determined when the initial conditions \(y(t_0) = y_0, y'(t_0) = y_1\) are provided. Therefore \(y(t)\) is not periodic Moreover, since \(\beta >0\) and \(0<\sqrt{\beta^2- \omega^2} < \beta\text{,}\) it follows that both \(-\beta + \sqrt{\beta^2- \omega^2}\) and \(-\beta - \sqrt{\beta^2- \omega^2}\) are negative. Consequently, we have
      \begin{equation*} \lim\limits_{t\to\infty} e^{(-\beta + \sqrt{\beta^2- \omega^2})t}=0 \quad \mbox{and} \quad \lim\limits_{t\to\infty}e^{(-\beta - \sqrt{\beta^2- \omega^2})t}=0. \end{equation*}
      It then follows that
      \begin{equation*} \lim\limits_{t\to\infty} y(t)=0. \end{equation*}
      We can show that the mass passes through the equilibrium position at most once by setting \(y(t) = 0\) and finding the time \(t\) at which this happens.
      🔗
      Regarding the velocity of the mass, we find
      \begin{equation*} \begin{split} y'(t) &= c_1 (-\beta + \sqrt{\beta^2- \omega^2})e^{(-\beta + \sqrt{\beta^2- \omega^2})t}\\ &+ c_2(-\beta - \sqrt{\beta^2- \omega^2}) e^{(-\beta - \sqrt{\beta^2- \omega^2})t}, \end{split} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Again, we observe that
      \begin{equation*} \lim\limits_{t\to\infty} y'(t)=0. \end{equation*}
      We can also show that the mass reaches an extreme position at most once by setting \(y'(t) = 0\) and finding the time \(t\) at which this happens.Since \(\beta = b/(2m)\) and \(\omega^2 = k/m,\) \(\beta > \omega\) is equivalent to \(b> 2\sqrt{mk}.\) We conclude that when the spring force is too weak to counteract a strong damping damping force, the mass approaches the equilibrium position with no oscillation and its velocity approaches zero, by changing its sign at most once, as \(t\) goes to \(\infty\text{.}\) In this case, the mass-spring system is said to be overdamped.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Critically Damped Motion: When \(\beta = \omega\text{,}\) (3.5.11) yields \(r = - \beta, -\beta,\) so that the general solution of (3.5.10) is
      \begin{equation*} y(t) = c_1 e^{-\beta t}+ c_2 te^{-\beta t} = (c_1+ c_2t) e^{-\beta t}, \end{equation*}
      where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants which can be determined when the initial conditions \(y(t_0) = y_0, y'(t_0) = y_1\) are known. The velocity of the mass is given by
      \begin{equation*} y'(t) = -\beta (c_1 + tc_2) e^{-\beta t} + c_2 e^{-\beta t} = (c_2 - \beta c_1- \beta c_2 t) e^{-\beta t}. \end{equation*}
      Since \(\beta >0,\) we find \(e^{-\beta t}\to 0\) and \(t e^{-\beta t}\to 0\) as \(t\to\infty.\) These imply that
      \begin{equation*} \lim\limits_{t\to\infty} y(t) = 0 \qquad \mbox{ and }\qquad \lim\limits_{t\to\infty} y'(t) = 0. \end{equation*}
      As note earlier in the case of an overdamped motion, the mass in this case can also pass thorough the equilibrium at most once and can reach an extreme position at most once while its velocity changes its sign. As \(t\) goes to \(\infty,\) the mass approaches its equilibrium position and its velocity also tends to zero.
      🔗
      Since \(\beta = b/(2m)\) and \(\omega^2 = k/m,\) \(\beta = \omega\) is equivalent to \(b= 2\sqrt{mk}.\) It follows from the case of underdamped motion that when the damping coefficient \(b\) is even slightly less than \(2\sqrt{mk},\) the mass undergoes an oscillatory motion. The spring-mass system with the smallest damping coefficient \(b\) that maintains a nonoscillatory motion is to be critically damped.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    III. Forced Undamped Motion (\(b=0,\;F(t) \ne 0\)).

    When \(b=0\) (no damping) and \(F(t) \ne 0\) (with external force), the spring-mass system is governed by the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} my''(t) + ky(t) = F(t). \end{equation*}
    which, with \(w^2 = k/m\) and \(f(t) = F(t)/m,\) becomes
    \begin{equation} y''(t) + \omega^2y(t) = f(t).\tag{3.5.12} \end{equation}
    🔗
    The nonhomogeneous equation (3.5.12) can be solved by using the methods of previous sections. The general solution is of the form
    \begin{equation} y(t) = y_h(t) + y_p(t) = c_1 \cos(\omega t) + c_2 \sin(\omega t) +y_p(t),\tag{3.5.13} \end{equation}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants and \(y_p(t)\) is a particular solution of (3.5.12). Recall that
    \begin{equation*} y_h(t) = c_1 \cos(\omega t) + c_2 \sin(\omega t) \end{equation*}
    is the general solution of the simple harmonic oscillator
    \begin{equation*} y''(t) + \omega^2 y(t)=0, \end{equation*}
    associated with (3.5.12).
    🔗
    Example 3.5.5.
    A \(10\)-kilogram mass attached to the end of a vertically hanging spring causes the spring to stretch \(39.2\) centimeters. At time \(t = 0\text{,}\) the spring-mass system is set into motion by an external force \(F(t) = 180\sin(10t)\text{,}\) where \(F(t)\) is measured in newtons and is positive in the downward direction, and time is measured in seconds. Determine the equation of motion and estimate the maximum excursion of the mass from its equilibrium position.
    🔗
    Solution.
    We let \(y(t)\) denote the displacement of the mass from the equilibrium position measured positively in the vertically downward direction.
    🔗
    We have \(m = 10~\text{kg}, s = 39.2~\text{cm} = 0.392~\text{m}, F(t) = 180\sin(10t)~\text{N}.\) For the mass in equilibrium, we have \(mg = ks\text{,}\) and so
    \begin{equation*} k = \frac{mg}{s} = \frac{10 \times 9.8}{0.392} = 250~\text{N/m}. \end{equation*}
    The natural frequency of the system is
    \begin{equation*} \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = 5 \text{ cycles in $2\pi$ seconds} = 5 \text{ rad/s} \end{equation*}
    The differential equation of motion is therefore
    \begin{equation*} 10y'' + 250y' = 180\sin(10t), \end{equation*}
    or equivalently,
    \begin{equation} y'' + 25y = 18\sin(5t).\tag{3.5.14} \end{equation}
    The general solution of \(y'' + 25y = 0\) is
    \begin{equation*} y_h(t) = c_1\cos(5t) + c_2\sin(5t), \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants. We find a particular solution \(y_p(t)\) of (3.5.14) by variation of parameters. Let
    \begin{equation*} y_p(t) = A\cos(10t) + B\sin(10t) \end{equation*}
    be a particular solution of (3.5.14), where \(A\) and \(B\) are to be determined. We find that \(y''_p(t) = - 100 y_p(t).\) Substituting \(y_p\) for \(y\) and \(y''_p\) for \(y''\) into (3.5.14) yields
    \begin{equation*} - 100 y_p(t)+25y_p(t) = 18 \sin(10t), \end{equation*}
    that is,
    \begin{equation*} -75A \cos(5t) - 75 B\sin(5t) =18 \sin(10t). \end{equation*}
    Equating the coefficients of the like terms, we have \(-75 A =0\) and \(-75 B = 18\text{,}\) that is, \(A=0\) and \(B = -6/25.\) Thus, we have
    \begin{equation*} y_p(t) = -\frac{6}{25} \sin(5t). \end{equation*}
    The general solution of (3.5.14) is
    \begin{equation*} y(t) = c_1\cos(5t) + c_2\sin(5t) - \frac{6}{25}\sin(10t), \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants. The initial conditions for \(y(t)\) are \(y(0) = 0, y'(0) = 0.\) Substituting these gives \(c_1 = 0\) and \(5c_2 - \frac{12}{5} = 0\text{,}\) i.e., \(c_1=0\) and \(c_2 = \frac{12}{25}\) Thus, we have
    \begin{equation*} y(t) = \frac{12}{25}\sin(5t) - \frac{6}{25} \sin(10t). \end{equation*}
    The graph of the solution is shown in Figure 3.5.6. Using the critical numbers and second derivative test, we can verify that the maximum occurs at \(t = \frac{2\pi}{15}.\) The maximum value is
    \begin{equation*} y\left(\frac{2\pi}{15}\right) = \frac{9\sqrt{3}}{25} \approx 0.6235. \end{equation*}

    🔗
    Resonance: An important aspect of analyzing undamped forced spring-mass systems is understanding how large (in absolute values) the values of \(y_p(t)\) can become when \(F(t)\) is sinusoidal. If the amplitude of \(y_p(t)\) grows without bound, the system is said to be in resonance. In practical applications, resonance may be undesirable because it can lead to excessive energy buildup and potential system failure. Since the system (3.5.12) is undamped, the only way energy can dissipate from the system is by the nature of the source term \(F(t).\) However, it is possible that the energy buildup can happen in the system even when \(F(t)\) is sign-changing. The following example demonstrates how resonance occurs.
    🔗
    With the external force in Example 3.5.5 replaced with \(F(t) = 180\sin(5t)\text{,}\) determine the equation of motion, analyze the amplitude of the resulting motion, and verify that the system exhibits resonance.
    🔗
    Similarly to the solution in Example 3.5.5, the initial value problem for the current example is
    \begin{equation} y'' + 25y = 18\sin(5t), \quad y(0) =0,\; y'(0) = 0.\tag{3.5.15} \end{equation}
    Recall from Example 3.5.5 that the natural frequency of the system is \(\omega = 10.\) We note here that the frequency of the external force \(\gamma =10\) matches the natural frequency \(\omega = 10.\) The general solution of \(y'' + 25y = 0\) is
    \begin{equation*} y_h(t) = c_1\cos(5t) + c_2\sin(5t), \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants. We find a particular solution \(y_p(t)\) of (3.5.15) by variation of parameters. Let
    \begin{equation*} y_p(t) = u_1(t)\cos(5t) + u_2(t)\sin(5t), \end{equation*}
    where \(u_1'(t)\) and \(u_2'(t)\) satisfy
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} u_1'(t)\cos(5t) + u_2'(t)\sin(5t) = 0, \\[4pt] -5\,u_1'(t)\sin(5t) + 5u_2'(t)\cos(5t) = 18\sin(5t). \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Solving this system gives
    \begin{equation*} u_1'(t) = -\frac{18}{5}\sin^2(5t) =-\frac{9}{5}(1-\cos(10t)), \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} u_2'(t) = \frac{18}{5}\sin(5t)\cos(5t) =\frac{9}{5}\sin(10t). \end{equation*}
    Integrating yields
    \begin{equation*} u_1(t) = -\frac{9}{5}t + \frac{9}{50} \sin(10t), \qquad u_2(t) = -\frac{9}{50} \cos(10t). \end{equation*}
    Hence,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} y_p(t) &= u_1\cos(5t) + u_2\sin(5t) \\[2pt] &= -\frac{9}{5}t \cos(5t) + \frac{9}{50}\sin(10t) \cos(5t) -\frac{9}{50}\cos(10t)\sin(5t)\\[2pt] &=-\frac{9}{5}t \cos(5t) + \frac{9}{50}\sin(5t). \\[2pt] \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    The general solution is
    \begin{equation*} y(t) = c_1\cos(5t) + c_2\sin(5t) - \frac{9}{5}t\cos(10t), \end{equation*}
    \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are constants to be determined so that \(y(0) = 0, y'(0) = 0.\) Substituting these gives \(c_1 = 0\) and \(5c_2 - \frac{9}{5} = 0\text{,}\) i.e., \(c_2 = \frac{9}{25}\) Thus, we have
    \begin{equation*} y(t) = -\frac{9}{5}t\cos(5t) + \frac{9}{25}\sin(5t). \end{equation*}
    It is evident that the amplitude \(a(t)\) of \(y(t)\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} a(t)=\frac{ 9}{5}\sqrt{t^2+\frac{1}{25}} \end{equation*}
    which shows that \(\lvert y(t) \rvert\) grows without bound as \(t\to\infty.\) Hence, the system exhibits resonance in this case, where the forcing frequency coincides the natural frequency-illustrating the classical example of resonance in an undamped forced spring-mass system. The plots of the solution \(y(t)\) and amplitude \(a(t)\) are shown in Figure 3.5.7.
