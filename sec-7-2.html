    Section 6.2 Partial Fraction Decomposition

    Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be general polynomials in \(x,\) and let \(m\) and \(n\) be the degrees of \(P\) and \(Q\text{,}\) respectively. Then the rational function
    \begin{equation*} R(x)=\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \end{equation*}
    is said to be proper if \(m<n\) and improper if \(m\ge n.\) When \(R(x)\) is improper, we perform polynomial division to obtain
    \begin{equation*} R(x)=q(x)+\frac{r(x)}{Q(x)}, \end{equation*}
    where \(q(x)\) is the quotient polynomial of degree \(m-n,\) and \(r(x)\) is called the remainder polynomial and it is of degree less than \(n.\) For example, if \(P(x) = x^{3}, Q(x) = x^{2}-1,\) then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^{3}+2}{x^{2}-1}= x+ \frac{x+2}{x^{2}-1}, \end{equation*}
    so that \(q(x) = x\) and \(r(x) = x+2.\)
    🔗
    After performing polynomial division, the term \(q(x)\) is already a polynomial and requires no decomposition. The term \(\dfrac{r(x)}{Q(x)}\) is a proper rational function and the only part of \(R(x)\) that is usually decomposed into partial fractions. Therefore, in what follows, we assume that \(R(x)\) itself is proper. The method of partial fraction decomposition then depends entirely on the factorization of the denominator \(Q(x)\text{.}\) We now examine all possible cases that may occur.
    🔗

    Subsection 6.2.1 Linear Factors \(x-a\)

    If the denominator contains distinct linear factors,
    \begin{equation*} Q(x)=(x-a_{1})(x-a_{2})\cdots(x-a_{n}), \end{equation*}
    then the decomposition has the form
    \begin{equation*} \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}= \frac{A_{1}}{x-a_{1}}+\frac{A_{2}}{x-a_{2}}+\cdots+\frac{A_{n}}{x-a_{n}}, \end{equation*}
    where \(A_{1}, \dots, A_{n}\) are constants to be determined. Let us illustrate this with an example below.
    🔗

    Example 6.2.1.

    Perform partial fraction decomposition of
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+5}{(x-1)(x+2)}. \end{equation*}
    Suppose there are constants \(A\) and \(B\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+5}{(x-1)(x+2)}= \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{B}{x+2}. \end{equation*}
    Multiplying through by \((x-1)(x+2)\) gives
    \begin{equation*} 3x+5 = A(x+2)+B(x-1) \end{equation*}
    which must be an identity in \(x.\) For convenience, we do allow \(x=1\) or \(x=-2\) in this identity. We find \(A=2\text{,}\) \(B=1\text{,}\) so
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+5}{(x-1)(x+2)}= \frac{2}{x-1}+\frac{1}{x+2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 6.2.2 Repeated Linear Factors \((x-a)^{n}\)

    If
    \begin{equation*} Q(x)=(x-a)^{n}, \end{equation*}
    then the decomposition includes every power of the linear factor:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{P(x)}{(x-a)^{n}}= \frac{A_{1}}{x-a}+ \frac{A_{2}}{(x-a)^{2}}+ \cdots + \frac{A_{n}}{(x-a)^{n}}, \end{equation*}
    \(A_{1}, \dots, A_{n}\) are constants to be determined.
    🔗

    Example 6.2.2.

    Perform the partial fraction decomposition of
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x+1}{(x-1)^{2}}. \end{equation*}
    Suppose there exist constants \(A\) and \(B\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x+1}{(x-1)^{2}}= \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{B}{(x-1)^{2}}. \end{equation*}
    Multiplying through by \((x-1)^{2}\) gives
    \begin{equation*} x+1 = A(x-1) + B. \end{equation*}
    We find \(A=1\) and \(B=2.\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x+1}{(x-1)^{2}}= \frac{1}{x-1}+\frac{2}{(x-1)^{2}}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 6.2.3 Irreducible Quadratic Factors \(x^{2}+bx+c\)

    Suppose that \(Q(x)\) contains a factor \(x^{2}+bx+c\) that is irreducible over the real numbers. The partial fraction decomposition of \(\ds\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\) contains a term of the form
    \begin{equation*} \frac{Ax+B}{x^{2}+bx+c}, \end{equation*}
    where \(A\) and \(B\) are constants to be determined. We illustrate this in the next an example.
    🔗

    Example 6.2.3.

    To perform the partial fraction decomposition of
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x^{2}+1)(x-2)}, \end{equation*}
    we find \(A\text{,}\)\(B\) and \(C\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x^{2}+1)(x-2)}= \frac{Ax+B}{x^{2}+1}+ \frac{C}{x-2}. \end{equation*}
    Notice the term \(\dfrac{C}{x-2}\) corresponding to the linear factor \(x-2\) of the denominator. Multiplying through by \((x^{2}+1)(x-2)\) yields
    \begin{equation*} x = (Ax+B)(x-2) + C(x^{2}+1). \end{equation*}
    Comparing coefficients gives
    \begin{equation*} A = \dfrac13, \qquad B = \dfrac23, \qquad C = -\dfrac13, \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x^{2}+1)(x-2)}= \frac{\tfrac13 x + \tfrac23}{x^{2}+1}- \frac{1}{3(x-2)}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 6.2.4 Repeated Irreducible Quadratic Factors

    If \(Q(x) = (x^{2}+bx+c)^{k},\) where the quadratic \(x^{2}+bx+c\) is irreducible and repeated with multiplicity \(k,\) then the decomposition \(\ds\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\) is of the form
    \begin{equation*} \frac{P(x)}{(x^{2}+bx+c)^{k}}= \frac{A_{1}x+B_{1}}{x^{2}+bx+c}+ \frac{A_{2}x+B_{2}}{(x^{2}+bx+c)^{2}}+ \cdots + \frac{A_{k}x+B_{k}}{(x^{2}+bx+c)^{k}}, \end{equation*}
    with the constants \(A_{1}, \dots, A_{k}\) and \(B_{1}, \dots, B_{k}\) are to be determined. We illustrate this in the next example.
    🔗

    Example 6.2.4.

    To perform the partial fraction decomposition of
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+5}{(x^{2}+1)^{2}}, \end{equation*}
    we find constants \(A, B, C, D\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+5}{(x^{2}+1)^{2}}= \frac{Ax+B}{x^{2}+1}+ \frac{Cx+D}{(x^{2}+1)^{2}}. \end{equation*}
    Multiplying both sides by \((x^{2}+1)^{2}\) gives
    \begin{equation*} 3x+5 = (Ax+B)(x^{2}+1) + (Cx+D). \end{equation*}
    Expanding and matching coefficients yields
    \begin{equation*} A=0,\quad B=3,\quad C=-3,\quad D=5, \end{equation*}
    and hence
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+5}{(x^{2}+1)^{2}}= \frac{3}{x^{2}+1}+ \frac{-3x+5}{(x^{2}+1)^{2}}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 6.2.5 Mixed Factors

    If the denominator \(Q(x)\) factors as

    The symbol \(\prod_{i=1}^{\ell} p_{i}\) denotes product \(p_{1}p_{2} \dots p_{\ell}\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} Q(x)= \prod_{i=1}^{\ell_1}(x-a_{i})^{k_i}\prod_{j=1}^{\ell_2}(x^{2} + b_{j}x + c_{j})^{m_j}, \end{equation*}
    then the full partial fraction decomposition of \(\ds\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\) contains terms
    🔗
    • \(\dfrac{A}{x-a}\text{,}\) \(\dfrac{A}{(x-a)^2}\text{,}\) etc., and
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\dfrac{Ax+B}{x^2+bx+c}\text{,}\) \(\dfrac{Ax+B}{(x^2+bx+c)^2}\text{,}\) etc.
      🔗
      🔗
    Let us illustrate this in the next example
    🔗

    Example 6.2.5.

    To perform the partial fraction decomposition of
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2x^{2}+3x+1}{(x-1)(x^{2}+1)}, \end{equation*}
    we find constants \(A, B, C\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2x^{2}+3x+1}{(x-1)(x^{2}+1)}= \frac{A}{x-1}+ \frac{Bx+C}{x^{2}+1}. \end{equation*}
    Multiplying both sides by \((x-1)(x^{2}+1)\) gives
    \begin{equation*} 2x^{2}+3x+1 = A(x^{2}+1) + (Bx+C)(x-1). \end{equation*}
    Expanding and comparing coefficients yields
    \begin{equation*} A = 1,\qquad B = 1,\qquad C = 2, \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2x^{2}+3x+1}{(x-1)(x^{2}+1)}= \frac{1}{x-1}+ \frac{x+2}{x^{2}+1}. \end{equation*}
