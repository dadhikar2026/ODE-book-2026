    Section 5.3 Derivatives and Integrals of Laplace Transforms

    The evaluation of Laplace transforms and inverse Laplace transforms can often be carried out efficiently by applying differentiation or integration techniques to certain Laplace transforms.
    🔗
    Let \(f\) be a piecewise continuous function on \([0, \infty)\) and of exponential order as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) Using the Leibniz rule from Theorem 6.1.2 in Section 6.1 for differentiating under the integral sign, we have
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} F'(s) &= \frac{d}{ds}\int_0^\infty e^{-st} f(t)\, dt\\ &= \int_0^\infty (-t) e^{-st} f(t)\, dt\\ &= -\int_0^\infty e^{-st} \big[tf(t)\big]\, dt\\ &=-\mathscr{L}\{tf(t)\}. \end{split} \end{equation*}
    Thus, we have
    \begin{equation*} \mathscr{L}\{tf(t)\} =- F'(s). \end{equation*}
    In a similar fashion, we obtain
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathscr{L}\{t^2f(t)\}= (-1)^2F''(s). \end{equation*}
    Repeating this procedure inductively we obtain
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \boxed{ \mathscr{L}\{t^nf(t)\} =(-1)^nF^{(n)}(s)}\tag{5.3.1} \end{equation}
    for all positive integers \(n\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 5.3.1.

    Find the Laplace transform \(\mathscr{L}\{t\sin(\omega t)\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} \mathscr{L}\{t\sin(\omega t)\} &= -\frac{d}{ds}\mathscr{L}\{\sin(\omega t)\}\\ &= -\frac{d}{ds}\left(\frac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2}\right)\\ &= \frac{2\omega s}{(s^2+\omega^2)^2}. \end{split} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.3.2.

    Find the Laplace transform \(\mathscr{L}\{t^2\sin(\omega t)\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} \mathscr{L}\{t^2\sin(\omega t)\} &= \frac{d^2}{ds^2}\mathscr{L}\{\sin(\omega t)\}\\ &= \frac{d^2}{ds^2}\left(\frac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2}\right)\\ &= \frac{2\omega(3s^2-\omega^2)}{(s^2+\omega^2)^3}. \end{split} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Let us discuss about what happens when you integrate a Laplace transform. Let \(f\) be a piecewise continuous function of exponential order as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) Suppose \(s_0\) is a real number such that \(F(s)\) exists for all \(s>s_0.\) To compute \(\mathscr{L}\{f(t)/t\}\text{,}\) let \(g(t) = f(t)/t\) so that \(t g(t) =f(t).\) Then, in view of (5.3.1), we obtain
    \begin{equation*} G'(s) = -F(s). \end{equation*}
    Consequently, we have
    \begin{equation*} G(s) = -\int_s^{\infty} G'(p)\; dp = \int_s^\infty F(p)\, dp. \end{equation*}
    Here, we have considered only those \(f\) for which the Laplace transform \(G(s)\) of \(g\) satisfies the limiting property that \(G(s) \to 0\) as \(s\to \infty.\) Thus, we have
    \begin{equation} \boxed{\mathscr{L}\left\{\frac{f(t)}{t}\right\}(s) = \int_s^\infty F(p)\, dp.} \tag{5.3.2} \end{equation}
    Moreover, we can use (5.3.2) to determine the existence of the Laplace transform of \(f(t).\) For instance, if the integral on the right-hand side of (5.3.2) diverges for all \(s>s_0,\) then the Laplace transform of \(f(t)/t\) does not exist for any \(s>s_0.\)
