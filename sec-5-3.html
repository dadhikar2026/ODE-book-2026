    Section 5.3 Derivatives and Integrals of Laplace Transforms

    The evaluation of Laplace transforms and inverse Laplace transforms can often be carried out efficiently by applying differentiation or integration techniques to certain Laplace transforms.
    🔗

    Subsection 5.3.1 Derivatives of Laplace Transforms

    Let \(f\) be a piecewise continuous function on \([0, \infty)\) and of exponential order as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) Using the Leibniz rule from Theorem 6.1.2 in Section 6.1 for differentiating under the integral sign, we have
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} F'(s) &= \frac{d}{ds}\int_0^\infty e^{-st} f(t)\, dt\\ &= \int_0^\infty (-t) e^{-st} f(t)\, dt\\ &= -\int_0^\infty e^{-st} \big[tf(t)\big]\, dt\\ &=-\mathcal {L}\{tf(t)\}. \end{split} \end{equation*}
    Thus, we have
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal {L}\{tf(t)\} =- F'(s). \end{equation*}
    In a similar fashion, we obtain
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal {L}\{t^2f(t)\}= (-1)^2F''(s). \end{equation*}
    Repeating this procedure inductively we obtain
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \boxed{ \mathcal {L}\{t^nf(t)\} =(-1)^nF^{(n)}(s)}\tag{5.3.1} \end{equation}
    for all positive integers \(n\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 5.3.1.

    Find the Laplace transform \(\mathcal {L}\{t\sin(\omega t)\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We have
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} \mathcal {L}\{t\sin(\omega t)\} &= -\frac{d}{ds}\mathcal {L}\{\sin(\omega t)\}\\ &= -\frac{d}{ds}\left(\frac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2}\right)\\ &= \frac{2\omega s}{(s^2+\omega^2)^2}. \end{split} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.3.2.

    Find the Laplace transform \(\mathcal {L}\{t^2\sin(\omega t)\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We have
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} \mathcal {L}\{t^2\sin(\omega t)\} &= \frac{d^2}{ds^2}\mathcal {L}\{\sin(\omega t)\}\\ &= \frac{d^2}{ds^2}\left(\frac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2}\right)\\ &= \frac{2\omega(3s^2-\omega^2)}{(s^2+\omega^2)^3}. \end{split} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 5.3.2 Integrals of Laplace Transforms

    Let us discuss about what happens when you integrate a Laplace transform. Let \(f\) be a piecewise continuous function of exponential order as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) Suppose \(s_0\) is a real number such that \(F(s)\) exists for all \(s>s_0.\) To compute \(\mathcal {L}\{f(t)/t\}\text{,}\) let \(g(t) = f(t)/t\) so that \(t g(t) =f(t).\) Then, in view of (5.3.1), we obtain
    \begin{equation*} G'(s) = -F(s). \end{equation*}
    Consequently, we have
    \begin{equation*} G(s) = -\int_s^{\infty} G'(p)\; dp = \int_s^\infty F(p)\, dp. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Here, we have considered only those \(f\) for which the Laplace transform \(G(s)\) of \(g\) satisfies the limiting property that \(G(s) \to 0\) as \(s\to \infty.\) Thus, we have
    \begin{equation} \boxed{\mathcal {L}\left\{\frac{f(t)}{t}\right\}(s) = \int_s^\infty F(p)\, dp.}\tag{5.3.2} \end{equation}
    Moreover, we can use (5.3.2) to determine the existence of the Laplace transform of \(f(t)/t.\) For instance, if the integral on the right-hand side of (5.3.2) diverges for all \(s>s_0,\) then the Laplace transform of \(f(t)/t\) does not exist for any \(s>s_0.\)
    🔗
    From the definition of the Laplace transform, it follows that
    \begin{equation*} F(0) = \int_0^\infty f(t)\, dt. \end{equation*}
    provided the improper integral on the right-hand side converges.
    🔗

    Example 5.3.3.

    Compute \(\mathcal {L}\{\sin(\omega t)/t\}\) for positive values of \(\omega\) .
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} \mathcal {L}\left\{\frac{\sin(\omega t)}{t}\right\}(s) &= \int_s^\infty \frac{\omega}{p^2+\omega^2} dp\\ &= \left[\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{p}{\omega}\right)\right]_s^\infty\\ &= \frac{\pi}{2}-\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{s}{\omega}\right). \end{split} \end{equation*}
    Note that the improper integral
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^\infty \frac{\sin(\omega t)}{t}\, dt \end{equation*}
    converges and is equal to \(\pi/2.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.3.4.

    Compute \(\mathcal {L}\{\cos(\omega t)/t\}\) for positive values of \(\omega\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We have
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal {L}\left\{\cos(\omega t) \right\}(p) = \frac{p}{p^2+\omega^2}. \end{equation*}
    Since
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} \int_s^\infty \frac{p}{p^2+\omega^2} dp\\ &= \left[\frac{1}{2}\ln(p^2+\omega^2)\right]_s^\infty\\ &= \infty, \end{split} \end{equation*}
    it follows that that the improper integral
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^\infty \frac{\cos(\omega t)}{t}\, dt \end{equation*}
    diverges.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.3.5.

    Compute \(\mathcal {L}\left\{\frac{e^t-1}{t}\right\}.\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal {L}\left\{e^t-1\right\}(p) = \frac{1}{p-1} - \frac{1}{p}, \end{equation*}
    we have
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} \mathcal {L}\left\{\frac{e^t-1}{t}\right\}(s) &= \int_s^\infty \left(\frac{1}{p-1} - \frac{1}{p}\right) dp\\ &= \left[\ln\left(\frac{p-1}{p}\right)\right]_s^\infty\\ &= \ln\left(\frac{s}{s-1}\right). \end{split} \end{equation*}
    for \(s>1.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
