Example 3.1.10.

Determine whether \(1, e^{x}\) and \(x e^{x}\) are linearly independent on \((-\infty, \infty).\)
🔗
Solution.
Suppose there are constants \(c_1, c_2,\) and \(c_3\) that satisfy
\begin{equation} c_1 + c_2e^x + c_3 xe^x=0\tag{3.1.5} \end{equation}
for all \(x\) in \((-\infty, \infty).\) Again, can we have at least one of \(c_1, c_2, c_3\) nonzero? Differentiating (3.1.5) both sides with respect to \(x\) gives
\begin{equation*} c_2e^x + c_3 e^x+ c_2 x e^x=0 \end{equation*}
for all \(x\) in \((-\infty, \infty).\) Since \(e^x\) is never zero, we have
\begin{equation} c_2 + c_3 + c_3 x =0\tag{3.1.6} \end{equation}
for all \(x\) in \((-\infty, \infty).\) Differentiating (3.1.6) both sides with respect to \(x\) yields \(c_3=0.\) Using \(c_3=0\) in (3.1.6) yields \(c_2 = 0.\) Using \(c_2=c_3=0\) in (3.1.5), we get \(c_1=0.\) Therefore \(1, e^{x}\) and \(x e^{x}\)are linearly independent on \((-\infty, \infty).\)
