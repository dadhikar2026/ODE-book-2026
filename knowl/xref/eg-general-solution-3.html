Example 3.2.9.

Find the general solution of
\begin{equation*} 3y''+2y'+y = 0. \end{equation*}
🔗
Solution.
Assume a solution of the form \(y = e^{\lambda x}\text{.}\) Then \(y' = \lambda e^{\lambda x}\) and \(y'' = \lambda^2 e^{\lambda x}\text{.}\) Substituting into the differential equation gives
\begin{equation*} (3\lambda^2 + 2\lambda + 1)e^{\lambda x} = 0. \end{equation*}
Then the auxiliary equation is
\begin{equation*} 3\lambda^2 + 2\lambda + 1=0, \end{equation*}
whose complex roots are
\begin{equation*} \lambda = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{-8}}{6} = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{2} i}{3} = -\frac{1}{3} \pm i \frac{\sqrt{2}}{3}. \end{equation*}
Substituting \(\alpha = -\frac{1}{3}\) and \(\beta = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{3}\) in (3.2.16), the general solution is
\begin{equation*} y = e^{-x/3} \left( c_1 \cos \left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{3} x\right) + c_2 \sin \left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{3} x\right) \right), \end{equation*}
where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants.
