    Section 3.1 Introduction

    Objectives

    • Understand basics of \(n^\text{th}\) order linear differential equations
      🔗
    🔗
    The \(n^\text{th}\) order linear differential equations are of the form
    \begin{equation} a_n(x)y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}(x)y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = f(x)\tag{3.1.1} \end{equation}
    where the functions \(a_0(x), \dots, a_{n-1}(x), a_n(x)\) and \(f(x)\) are continuous on some interval \(I .\) We say that (3.1.1) is homogeneous on \(I\) if \(f(x) = 0\) for all \(x\) in \(I\) and nonhomogeneous on \(I\) if \(f(x)\ne 0\) for some \(x\) in \(I.\) We will usually have \(a_n(x) \ne 0\) on \(I.\) If \(a_n(x)=0\) for some point \(x\) of \(I,\) we will simply restrict our attention to a smaller interval, denoted again by \(I\text{,}\) on which \(a_n(x) \ne 0.\) With these consideration, (3.1.1) can be rewritten in the form
    \begin{equation} y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}(x)y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = f(x),\tag{3.1.2} \end{equation}
    \(a_0(x), \dots, a_{n-1}(x)\) and \(f(x)\) are continuous on some interval \(I .\) This is achieved by dividing both sides of (3.1.1) by \(a_n(x)\) to obtain
    \begin{equation*} y^{(n)}+\frac{a_{n-1}(x)}{a_n(x)}y^{(n-1)}+ \cdots+\frac{a_1(x)}{a_n(x)}y'+\frac{a_0(x)}{a_n(x)}y= \frac{f(x)}{a_n(x)} \end{equation*}
    and redefining the functions
    \begin{equation*} \frac{a_{n-1}(x)}{a_n(x)}, \dots,\frac{a_1(x)}{a_n(x)}, \frac{a_0(x)}{a_n(x)}, \frac{f(x)}{a_n(x)} \end{equation*}
    as
    \begin{equation*} a_{n-1}(x), \dots,a_1(x), a_0(x), f(x), \end{equation*}
    respectively.
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.1.

    A function \(y = \phi(x)\) defined on \(I\) is said to be a solution of (3.1.2) if
    \begin{equation*} \phi^{(n)}(x) + a_{n-1}(x)\phi^{(n-1)}(x) + \cdots + a_1(x)\phi'(x) + a_0(x)\phi(x) = f(x) \end{equation*}
    for all \(x\) in \(I,\) that is, if (3.1.2) is satisfied for all \(x\) in \(I\) when we substitute \(\phi(x)\) for \(y.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    In Definition 3.1.1, we note the following.
    🔗
    1. Derivatives of \(\phi(x)\) at the endpoints (if any) of \(I\) are understood as one-sided.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Solutions \(y = \phi(x)\) may be complex-valued, though real solutions are preferred in applications. For \(\phi(x) = \phi_1(x) + i \phi_2(x)\) with real \(\phi_1\) and \(\phi_2\text{,}\)
      \begin{equation*} \phi'(x) = \phi_1'(x) + i \phi_2'(x), \end{equation*}
      where \(i = \sqrt{-1}\text{,}\) and higher derivatives are computed similarly.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.2.

    Let \(a_0(x), \dots, a_{n-1}(x), f(x)\) be functions defined on some interval \(I\) , \(x_0\) be a point in \(I\text{,}\) and \(y_1,\dots, y_{n-1}\) be fixed numbers. The \(n^\text{th}\) order linear differential equation
    \begin{equation*} y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}(x)y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = f(x) \end{equation*}
    subject to the conditions (called initial conditions)
    \begin{equation*} \\ y(x_0) = y_0,\; y'(x_0) = y_1, \dots, y^{(n-1)}(x_0) = y_{n-1}, \end{equation*}
    is called an initial value problem (IVP).
    🔗
    🔗
    It is essential to know when an initial value problem has a solution if \(a_0(x), \dots, a_{n-1}(x), f(x)\) are continuous on some interval \(I\) containing \(x_0\text{.}\) This is addressed by the existence and uniqueness theorem stated below, whose proof requires advanced calculus and is beyond the scope of this book.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.1.3. (Existence and Uniqueness).

    Let \(a_0(x), \dots, a_{n-1}(x), f(x)\) be continuous functions on some interval \(I\) , \(x_0\) be a point in \(I\text{,}\) and \(y_1,\dots, y_{n-1}\) be fixed numbers. Then the IVP
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}(x)y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = f(x)\\ y(x_0) = y_0,\; y'(x_0) = y_1, \dots, y^{(n-1)}(x_0) = y_{n-1}, \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    has a unique solution \(y = \phi(x)\) defined on \(I.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.1.4.

    When \(f(x)=0\) for all \(x\) in \(I\) and \(y_1=y_2 =\cdots = y_{n-1} = 0\text{,}\) it follows from Theorem 3.1.3 that the function \(\phi(x) =0\) for all \(x\) in \(I\) is the unique solution of the IVP. By the uniqueness, if at least one of \(y_1, \dots, y_{n-1}\) is nonzero, then the unique solution of the IVP in Theorem 3.1.3 must be a nonzero function.
    🔗
    🔗
    The superposition principle discussed below provides a general procedure for constructing new solutions from known solutions.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.1.5. (Superposition Principle).

    Suppose that \(y_1, \dots, y_k\) are solutions of the homogeneous linear equation
    \begin{equation*} y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}(x)y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = 0, \end{equation*}
    where \(a_0(x), \dots, a_{n-1}(x)\) are continuous on some interval \(I .\) Then, for any constants \(c_1, \dots, c_k\) (possibly complex), the function
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = c_1y_1(x) + \cdots+c_k y_k(x) = \sum_{j=1}^k c_j y_j(x) \end{equation*}
    is a solution on \(I.\) The expression \(\sum_{j=1}^k c_j y_j(x)\) is called a linear combination (or superposition) of \(y_1, \dots, y_k,\) with weights \(c_1, \dots, c_k.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    We treat the special case \(n=2\) here for clarity of this proof and leave the general case as an exercise. Let \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) be solutions on \(I\) of
    \begin{equation} y'' + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = 0,\tag{3.1.3} \end{equation}
    and let \(y = c_1 y_1 + c_2y_2\) for constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2.\) Then
    \begin{equation*} y' = c_1 y'_1 + c_2y'_2\quad\mbox{and}\quad y'' = c_1 y''_1 + c_2y''_2 \end{equation*}
    on \(I.\) We note that the differentiation rules also apply when \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are complex. Substituting these into the left side of the differential equation gives
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} &y'' + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y\\ &=c_1\big(y''_1 + a_1(x)y'_1 + a_0(x)y_1\big)+c_2\big(y''_2 + a_1(x)y'_2 + a_0(x)y_2\big)\\ \end{split} \end{equation*}
    on \(I.\) Since \(y_j\) satisfies
    \begin{equation*} y''_j + a_1(x)y'_j + a_0(x)y_j = 0 \end{equation*}
    on \(I\) for \(j = 1, 2,\) we obtain
    \begin{equation*} y'' + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y=0. \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(y= c_1y_1 +c_2y_2\) is a solution of (3.1.3) on \(I.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.1.6.

    The superposition principle provides the following basic operations for constructing new solutions from known solutions.
    🔗
    1. If \(y_1\) is a solution, then \(cy_1\) is also a solution for any constant \(c\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are solutions, then so is \(y_1 +y_2.\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.7.

    We can verify that \(y_1 = \cos x\) and \(y_2 = \sin x\) are solutions of \(y''+y = 0,\) which is a homogeneous linear equation. Moreover, \(y= 7 \cos x\) and \(y = 2\cos x -3 \sin x\) are also solutions. In fact, \(y = c_1 \cos x\) and \(y = c_1 \cos x+c_2 \sin x\) are solutions for any constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    When building new solutions of a homogeneous linear differential equation from a collection of known solutions, it is desirable to use a minimal set of solutions as building blocks. Even if several solutions are available, we may not actually need many of them, and some solutions can be discarded based on how they are related to the remaining solutions. This important technique is addressed in the next subsection.
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.1 Linear Independence

    Two functions \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) defined on an interval \(I\) are said to be linearly independent on \(I\) if neither function is a constant multiple of the other. For instance, the functions \(e^x\) and \(e^{-x}\) are linearly independent on \((-\infty, \infty)\text{,}\) as are \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\) on \((-\infty, \infty)\text{.}\) The concept of linear independence of functions extends naturally to any finite collection of functions, as formalized in the following definition.
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.8.

    The functions \(y_1, \dots, y_k\) defined on an interval \(I\text{,}\) where \(k\) is a positive integer, are said to be linearly independent on \(I\) if the relation
    \begin{equation*} c_1 y_1(x) + \cdots + c_k y_k(x) = 0 \end{equation*}
    holds for all \(x\) in \(I\) only when the constants \(c_1, \dots, c_k\) satisfy \(c_1 = \cdots = c_k = 0\text{.}\) If this condition does not hold, that is, if there exist constants \(c_1, \dots, c_k\text{,}\) not all zero, satisfying the above relation for all \(x\) in \(I\text{,}\) then the functions \(y_1, \dots, y_k\) are said to be linearly dependent on \(I\).
    🔗
    🔗
    We say that the set \(\{y_1, \dots, y_k\}\) is linearly independent on \(I\) if the functions \(y_1, \dots, y_k\) are linearly independent on \(I.\) Similarly, we say that the set \(\{y_1, \dots, y_k\}\) is linearly dependent on \(I\) if the functions \(y_1, \dots, y_k\) are linearly dependent on \(I.\)
    🔗
    The linear dependence or independence of a set of functions on an interval may change when the interval is changed or when functions are added or removed. It follows directly from the definition that if the set \(\{y_1, \dots, y_k\}\) is linearly independent on \(I,\) then it remains linearly independent on any larger interval \(J\) containing \(I,\) provided the functions are defined on \(J.\) Similarly, if the set \(\{y_1, \dots, y_k\}\) is linearly dependent on \(I,\) it will also be linearly dependent on every subinterval \(J\) of \(I.\)
    🔗
    Moreover, using the definition above, any subset of a linearly independent set of functions is itself linearly independent, whereas a set that contains a linearly independent set can be linearly dependent. Similarly, a set containing a linearly dependent set of functions is necessarily linearly dependent, whereas a subset of a linearly dependent set can be linearly independent.
    🔗
    Suppose the functions \(y_1, \dots, y_k\) are linearly dependent on an interval \(I\text{.}\) Then there exist constants \(c_1, \dots, c_k\text{,}\) not all zero, such that
    \begin{equation} c_1 y_1(x) + \cdots + c_k y_k(x) = 0\tag{3.1.4} \end{equation}
    for all \(x\) in \(I.\) Since the constants are not all zero, at least one of them must be nonzero. Suppose, for instance, that \(c_1\ne 0.\) Then, from (3.1.4), we can express
    \begin{equation*} y_1(x) = \left(-\frac{c_2}{c_1}\right) y_2(x) + \left(-\frac{c_3}{c_1}\right) y_3(x) + \cdots + \left(-\frac{c_k}{c_1}\right) y_k(x) \end{equation*}
    on \(I\text{.}\) This shows that \(y_1\) is a linear combination of the remaining functions. Similarly, if \(c_2 \ne 0,\) then
    \begin{equation*} y_2(x) = \left(-\frac{c_1}{c_2}\right) y_1(x) + \left(-\frac{c_3}{c_2}\right) y_3(x) + \cdots + \left(-\frac{c_k}{c_2}\right) y_k(x) \end{equation*}
    on \(I\text{,}\) and so \(y_2\) is a linear combination of the remaining functions. In general, for any integer \(k_0\) with \(2 \le k_0 \le k\) and \(c_{k_0} \ne 0,\) (3.1.4) implies that \(y_{k_0}\) is a linear combination of the remaining functions. Thus, if \(\{y_1, \dots, y_k\}\) is a linearly dependent set of functions \(y_1, \dots, y_k\) defined on \(I,\) then at least one of the functions can be expressed as a linear combination of the others. In particular, this means that any set \(\{y_1, \dots, y_k\}\) containing the zero function is necessarily linearly dependent.
    🔗
    From a linearly dependent set, it is often desirable to remove any function that can be expressed as a linear combination of the remaning functions. By repeatedly removing such functions, we eventually obtain a linearly independent set. The functions in this linearly independent set can then be used to construct new functions by forming linear combinations.
    🔗

    Example 3.1.9.

    Determine whether \(1, \cos^2x,\) and \(\sin^2x\) are linearly independent on \((-\infty, \infty).\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Suppose there are constants \(c_1, c_2,\) and \(c_3\) that satisfy
    \begin{equation*} c_1 + c_2\cos^2x + c_3\sin^2x=0 \end{equation*}
    for all \(x\) in \((-\infty, \infty).\) The main question here is: can we have at least one of \(c_1, c_2, c_3\) nonzero? In view of \(\cos^2 x+ \sin^2 x=1\text{,}\) we can take \(c_1 = -1, c_2=c_3 =1.\) Therefore \(1, \cos^2x,\) and \(\sin^2x\) are linearly dependent on \((-\infty, \infty).\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.10.

    Determine whether \(1, e^{x}\) and \(x e^{x}\) are linearly independent on \((-\infty, \infty).\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Suppose there are constants \(c_1, c_2,\) and \(c_3\) that satisfy
    \begin{equation} c_1 + c_2e^x + c_3 xe^x=0\tag{3.1.5} \end{equation}
    for all \(x\) in \((-\infty, \infty).\) Again, can we have at least one of \(c_1, c_2, c_3\) nonzero? Differentiating (3.1.5) both sides with respect to \(x\) gives
    \begin{equation*} c_2e^x + c_3 e^x+ c_2 x e^x=0 \end{equation*}
    for all \(x\) in \((-\infty, \infty).\) Since \(e^x\) is never zero, we have
    \begin{equation} c_2 + c_3 + c_3 x =0\tag{3.1.6} \end{equation}
    for all \(x\) in \((-\infty, \infty).\) Differentiating (3.1.6) both sides with respect to \(x\) yields \(c_3=0.\) Using \(c_3=0\) in (3.1.6) yields \(c_2 = 0.\) Using \(c_2=c_3=0\) in (3.1.5), we get \(c_1=0.\) Therefore \(1, e^{x}\) and \(x e^{x}\)are linearly independent on \((-\infty, \infty).\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.1.2 The Wronskian

    The methods used in Example 3.1.9 and Example 3.1.10 to test the linear independence of functions can become cumbersome, especially when the functions involved are more complicated than those in the examples. In this book, we will typically need to determine whether a set of solutions of homogeneous linear differential equations is linearly independent. For such sets of solutions, the linear independence of solutions can be determined by using the Wronskian, a determinant involving solutions and their derivatives, introduced in 1812 by the Polish mathematician Józef Wroński.
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.11. (Wronskian).

    Let \(y_1, y_2, \dots, y_n\) be functions that are \(n-1\) times differentiable on an interval \(I.\) Their Wronskian, denoted by \(W(y_1, \dots, y_{n-1})(x)\) (or simply by \(W(x)\)), is a function on \(I\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} W(y_1,\dots,y_n)(x) = \begin{vmatrix} y_1(x) & y_2(x) & \cdots & y_n(x)\\ y_1'(x) & y_2'(x) & \cdots & y_n'(x)\\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\ y_1^{(n-1)}(x) & y_2^{(n-1)}(x) & \cdots & y_n^{(n-1)}(x) \end{vmatrix} \end{equation*}
    on \(I.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.12.

    Compute the Wronskian of \(y_1=e^x\) and \(y_2=xe^{-x}\) on \((-\infty, \infty).\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The Wronskian \(W(y_1,y_2)(x)\) of \(y_1=e^x\) and \(y_2=xe^{-x}\) is
    \begin{equation*} W(e^x,xe^{-x}) = \begin{vmatrix} y_1(x) & y_2(x) \\ y_1'(x) & y_2'(x) \\ \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} e^x & xe^{-x} \\ e^x & e^{-x}-xe^{-x} \\ \end{vmatrix} = 1-2x \end{equation*}
    on \((-\infty, \infty).\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The next theorem provides a Wronskian test for linear independence of differentiable functions.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.1.13. (Wronskian Test for Linear Independence).

    Let \(y_1, \dots, y_k\) be \(k-1\) times differentiable functions on an interval \(I\) with
    \begin{equation*} W(y_1, \dots, y_k)(x)\ne 0 \mbox{ on } I. \end{equation*}
    Then \(y_1, \dots, y_k\) are linearly independent on \(I.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    For simplicity, we treat the case \(k=2;\) the general case is analogous. Suppose that \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are differentiable functions on an interval \(I\) such that \(W(y_1, y_2)(x)\ne 0\) on \(I.\) Let \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) be constants such that
    \begin{equation} c_1 y_1(x) + c_2 y_2(x) = 0\tag{3.1.7} \end{equation}
    for all \(x\) in \(I.\) Differentiating gives
    \begin{equation} c_1 y_1'(x) + c_2 y_2'(x) = 0\tag{3.1.8} \end{equation}
    for all \(x\) in \(I.\) Since \(W(y_1,y_2)(x)\neq 0\) for every \(x\) in \(I,\) the linear system consisting of the (3.1.7) and (3.1.8) has the unique solution \(c_1=c_2=0\) for every \(x\) in \(I.\) Hence \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are linearly independent on \(I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.1.14.

    1. The conclusion of Theorem 3.1.13 holds even if \(W(y_1,\dots,y_k)(x_0)\neq 0\) at just one point \(x_0\) in \(I\text{.}\) The case \(k=2\) appears as (i) in Exercises 3.1.3.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The converse of Theorem 3.1.13 is not true. For example, take the differentiable functions \(y_1= x^2\) and \(y_2 = x\lvert x\rvert\) on \((-\infty, \infty).\) We know from Example that \(y_1, y_2\) are linearly independent on \((-\infty, \infty);\) however, their Wronskian
      \begin{equation*} W(x^2, x\lvert{x}\rvert) = \begin{vmatrix} x^2&x\lvert{x}\rvert\\ 2x &2\lvert{x}\rvert \end{vmatrix} =0 \end{equation*}
      on \((-\infty, \infty).\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. We will see in Theorem 3.2.1 for the case \(k=2\) and in Theorem 3.4.1 for the general case that the converse of Theorem 3.1.13 also holds if \(y_1, \dots, y_k\) are solutions of a \(k^\text{th}\) order homogeneous linear differential equation.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. A useful formula for the Wronskian of two solutions of a second order linear homogeneous equation is derived in Section 3.2. Problem Exercise 3.2.3.7 in Exercises 3.2.3 shows how the Wronskian can be used to determine a second linearly independent solution when one solution is known.
      🔗
      🔗
