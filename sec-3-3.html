    Section 3.3 Second Order Nonhomogeneous Linear Differential Equations

    Objectives

    • Understand the structure of the general solution of second order nonhomogeneous linear differential equations.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Learn methods for solving second order nonhomogeneous linear differential equations.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    In this section, we discuss methods for solving second order nonhomogeneous linear differential equations. The same ideas extend naturally to higher order equation (see Section 3.4). The following is concerning how the general solutions of nonhomogeneous linear differential equations must be formatted.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.3.1. (General Solution).

    Consider the nonhomogeneous equation
    \begin{equation} y'' + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = f(x),\tag{3.3.1} \end{equation}
    where \(a_1\text{,}\) \(a_0\) and \(f\) are continuous functions on an interval \(I\) and the associated homogeneous equation
    \begin{equation} y'' + a_1(x)y' + a_0(x)y = 0\tag{3.3.2} \end{equation}
    on \(I\text{.}\) Let \(y_h\) be the general solution of (3.3.2) and \(y_p\) be a particular solution of the nonhomogeneous equation (3.3.1) on \(I.\) Then the general solution of (3.3.1) is of the form
    \begin{equation} \boxed{y = y_h(x) +y_p(x)}\tag{3.3.3} \end{equation}
    on \(I.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(y_p\) be a particular solution of (3.3.1). Let \(y\) be any other solution of (3.3.1). Then the difference
    \begin{equation} \phi(x)=y(x) -y_p(x)\tag{3.3.4} \end{equation}
    is a solution of the homogeneous equation (3.3.2). In fact, for all \(x\) in \(I\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} &\phi''(x) + a_1(x)\phi'(x) + a_0(x)\phi(x)\\ &= \left(y''(x) -y_p''(x)\right)+a_1(x)\left(y'(x) -y_p'(x)\right)+a_0(x)\left(y(x) -y_p(x)\right)\\ &= \left(y''(x) + a_1(x)y'(x) + a_0(x)y(x)\right)\\ & \quad - \left(y_p''(x) + a_1(x)y_p'(x) + a_0(x)y_p(x)\right)\\ &=f(x)-f(x)\\ &=0. \end{split} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, \(\phi(x)\) takes the form of \(y_h(x)\) on \(I.\) It follows from (3.3.4) that
    \begin{equation*} y= y_h(x) + y_p(x)\quad \mbox{ on } I \end{equation*}
    is the general solution of (3.3.1).
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.3.2.

    Theorem 3.3.1 provides the fundamental structure for the general solution of nonhomogeneous linear differential equations. The function \(y_h\) in (3.3.3) is called the complementary function of the general solution, and it is given by
    \begin{equation*} y_h(x)=c_1 y_1(x) + c_2 y_2(x), \end{equation*}
    where \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) form a fundamental set of solutions for (3.3.2) and \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants.
    🔗
    🔗
    In practice, solving a nonhomogeneous equation begins with finding the complementary function by determining a fundamental set of solutions to the associated homogeneous equation. We then find a particular solution of the nonhomogeneous equation, and the sum of the two provides the general solution. Finding a particular solution is typically the more difficult step; we outline two procedures for finding it in this section.
    🔗

    Subsection 3.3.1 Variation of Parameters

    The variation of parameters is a systematic method for constructing a particular solution of a nonhomogeneous linear differential equation of the form
    \begin{equation} a_2(x) y'' + a_1(x) y' + a_0(x) y = g(x),\tag{3.3.5} \end{equation}
    where the forcing term \(g\) and coefficient functions \(a_0, a_1,\) and \(a_2\) are assumed to be continuous on an interval \(I\) with \(a_2(x)\ne 0\) on \(I.\) We will be working with scenarios in which \(a_2(x)\) is either only positive or only negative on \(I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To formulate the method, we put (3.3.5) in the standard form
    \begin{equation} y'' + a_1(x) y' + a_0(x) y = f(x),\tag{3.3.6} \end{equation}
    by first dividing both sides of (3.3.5) by \(a_2(x)\) to obtan
    \begin{equation*} y'' + \frac{a_1(x)}{a_2(x)} y' + \frac{a_0(x)}{a_2(x)} y = \frac{g(x)}{a_2(x)}, \end{equation*}
    and then redefining
    \begin{equation*} \frac{a_1(x)}{a_2(x)} \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{a_0(x)}{a_2(x)} \end{equation*}
    as \(a_1(x)\) and \(a_0(x)\text{,}\) respectively, and defining \(f(x) = \ds\frac{g(x)}{a_2(x)}.\)
    🔗
    Assume that \(\{ y_1, y_2 \}\) is a fundamental set of solutions on \(I\) for the homogeneous equation
    \begin{equation} y'' + a_1(x) y' + a_0(x) y = 0,\tag{3.3.7} \end{equation}
    associated to the nonhomogeneous equation (3.3.6). Then the general solution to (3.3.6) is
    \begin{equation} y_h(x) = c_1 y_1(x) + c_2 y_2(x),\tag{3.3.8} \end{equation}
    for all \(x\) in \(I\text{,}\) where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants.
    🔗
    To find a particular solution of the nonhomogeneous equation (3.3.6), the method of variation of parameters assumes a solution of the form
    \begin{equation*} y_p(x) = u_1(x) y_1(x) + u_2(x) y_2(x), \end{equation*}
    for all \(x\) in \(I\text{,}\) where \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) are functions to be determined. The choice of \(y_p\) reflects the name of the method: variation of parameters. The constants \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) in the solution \(y_h\) of the homogeneous equation (3.3.7) are replaced by the functions \(u_1(x)\) and \(u_2(x)\text{,}\) thereby varying the parameters in order to obtain a particular solution to (3.3.6). For the remainder of the discussion of the method, we suppress \(x\) in all functions to minimize distractions.
    🔗
    Differentiating \(y_p = u_1y_1 +u_2y_2\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} y_p' = u_1 y_1' +u_1' y_1 + u_2 y_2'+ u_2' y_2 = u_1 y_1' + u_2 y_2'+(u_1' y_1 + u_2' y_2) \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} y_p'' &= u_1 y_1'' +u_1' y_1' +u_2 y_2''+ u_2' y_2' + \big( u_1' y_1 + u_2' y_2 \big)'\\[4pt] &= u_1 y_1''+u_2 y_2'' +u_1' y_1' + u_2' y_2' + \big( u_1' y_1 + u_2' y_2 \big)' \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    We impose the condition
    \begin{equation*} u_1' y_1 + u_2' y_2 = 0, \end{equation*}
    which is trivially satisfied when \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) are constant functions. The condition simplifies the expressions for \(y_p'\) and \(y_p''\) as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} y_p' &= u_1 y_1' + u_2 y_2', \text{ and }\\ y_p'' &= u_1 y_1''+u_2 y_2'' +u_1' y_1' + u_2' y_2'. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Substituting \(y_p\text{,}\) \(y_p'\text{,}\) and \(y_p''\) for \(y, y',\) and \(y''\text{,}\) respectively, into (3.3.6) gives
    \begin{equation*} u_1' y_1' + u_1 y_1'' + u_2' y_2' + u_2 y_2''+a_1 u_1 y_1' + a_1 u_2 y_2' +a_0 u_1 y_1 + a_0 u_2 y_2 = f, \end{equation*}
    which can be rewritten as
    \begin{equation*} u_1 (y_1''+a_1 y_1'+a_0 y_1)+ u_2 (y_2''+a_1 y_2'+a_0 y_2) +u_1' y_1' + u_2' y_2' = f. \end{equation*}
    Since \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) satisfy the homogeneous equation (3.3.7), the expressions in parentheses are zero, and therefore we obtain the system
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} y_1 u_1' + y_2 u_2' = 0,\\ y_1' u_1' + y_2' u_2' = f. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    This system can be solved for \(u_1'\) and \(u_2'\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} u_1' = -\frac{y_2 f}{W}, \quad u_2' = \phantom{-}\frac{y_1 f}{W}, \end{equation*}
    where \(W = y_1 y_2' - y_2 y_1'\) is the Wronskian of \(y_1\) and \(y_2.\) By integrating, we obtain
    \begin{equation} u_1 = -\int \frac{y_2 f}{W} \, dx, \quad u_2 = \int \frac{y_1 f}{W} \, dx.\tag{3.3.9} \end{equation}
    Therefore, \(y_p\) is given by
    \begin{equation} y_p = -y_1 \int \frac{y_2 f}{W} \, dx + y_2 \int \frac{y_1 f}{W} \, dx.\tag{3.3.10} \end{equation}
    Consequently, the general solution of (3.3.6) is
    \begin{equation*} y = y_h + y_p = c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 - y_1 \int \frac{y_2 f}{W} \, dx + y_2 \int \frac{y_1 f}{W} \, dx. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Remark 3.3.3.

    The main advantage of the method of variation of parameters over the method of undetermined coefficients is that it is applicable to all non homogeneous linear differential equations, including the equations with variable coefficients. However, the evaluation of the integrals in (3.3.9) can become quite cumbersome even when \(f(x)\) involves only elementary functions, such as the ones treated with the method of undetermined coefficients discussed Subsection 3.3.2.
    🔗
    🔗
    We illustrate the method of variation of parameters with the following initial value problem which can be readily solved using the method of undetermined coefficients.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.4.

    Solve the initial value problem
    \begin{equation*} y'' + 8 y' + 16 y = 4 x,\quad y(0) = 0,\; y'(0) = 0 \end{equation*}
    by variation of parameters.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The auxiliary equation for the associated homogeneous equation is \(\lambda^2 + 8\lambda + 16 = 0\) which gives \(\lambda = -4, -4,\) and so the functions
    \begin{equation*} y_1(x) = e^{-4x}, \quad y_2(x) = x e^{-4x} \end{equation*}
    form a fundamental set solutions to \(y'' + 8 y' + 16 y=0.\) The Wronskian of \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) is
    \begin{equation*} W(x) = \begin{vmatrix} e^{-4x} & x e^{-4x} \\ -4 e^{-4x} & (1 - 4x) e^{-4x} \end{vmatrix} = e^{-8x} (1 - 4x + 4x) = e^{-8x}. \end{equation*}
    We note that the given nonhomogeneous equation is in the standard form with \(f(x) = 4x\) and therefore a particular solution by the method of variation of parameters is given by
    \begin{equation*} y_p = -y_1 \int \frac{y_2 f}{W} dx + y_2 \int \frac{y_1 f}{W} dx. \end{equation*}
    Then the integration by parts with the Kronecker method (see Figure 3.3.5) gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{y_2 f}{W} dx= \int \frac{4x^2 e^{-4x}}{e^{-8x}}\, dx =x^2 e^{4x} - \frac{1}{2} x e^{4x} + \frac{1}{8} e^{4x}. \end{equation*}
    In a similar manner, we also evaluate
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{y_1 f}{W} dx=\int \frac{4x e^{-4x}}{e^{-8x}}\, dx= \int 4x e^{4x} dx = x e^{4x} - \frac{1}{4} e^{4x}. \end{equation*}
    Then \(y_p\) becomes
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} y_p &= -e^{-4x} \left( x^2 e^{4x} - \frac{1}{2} x e^{4x} + \frac{1}{8} e^{4x} \right) + x e^{-4x}\left( x e^{4x} - \frac{1}{4} e^{4x} \right)\\ &=- x^2 + \frac{x}{2} - \frac{1}{8} + x^2 - \frac{x}{4}\\ &=\frac{x}{4} - \frac{1}{8}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    The general solution of the nonhomogeneous differential equation is
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = c_1 e^{-4x} + c_2 x e^{-4x} + \frac{x}{4} - \frac{1}{8}, \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants. Since \(y(0) = 0,\) we get \(c_1 = \frac{1}{8}.\) We find
    \begin{equation*} y'(x) = -4 c_1 e^{-4x} + c_2 e^{-4x} - 4 c_2 x e^{-4x} + \frac{1}{4}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(y'(0) =0,\) we have\(-4 c_1 + c_2 + \frac{1}{4} = 0.\) Using \(c_1= \frac{1}{8}\text{,}\) we get \(-\frac{1}{2} + c_2 + \frac{1}{4} = 0\text{,}\) so that \(c_2 = \frac{1}{4}.\) Hence the solution to the given initial value problem is
    \begin{equation*} y(x) = \frac{1}{8} e^{-4x} + \frac{1}{4} x e^{-4x} + \frac{x}{4} - \frac{1}{8} \end{equation*}
    on \((-\infty, \infty).\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.3.2 Method of Undetermined Coefficients

    We note that variation of parameters often leads to integrals that can be quite involved and sometimes unexpectedly time-consuming even for simple forcing terms such as \(f(x) = \sin x\text{.}\) For instance, to find a particular solution of
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 9y = \sin x, \end{equation*}
    one may instead guess a solution of the form \(y_p(x) = A\sin x\) (or a slightly more general form, \(y_p(x) = A\sin x +B\cos x,\) if needed). See Example 3.3.6 and Example 3.3.8 below. This strategy of guessing a trial function for \(y_p\) based on type of \(f(x)\) and determining the unknown constant coefficients in the trial function is known as the method of undetermined coefficients. However, a demerit of this method is that it becomes quite complicated when differential equations has variable coefficients.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.6.

    Find a particular solution of
    \begin{equation} y'' - 9y = \sin x\tag{3.3.11} \end{equation}
    by using the method of undetermined coefficients and write down the general solution.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let
    \begin{equation*} y_p = A\sin x \end{equation*}
    be a particular solution of the equation, where \(A\) is a constant to be determined. Differentiating yields
    \begin{equation*} y_p' = A\cos x, \quad y_p''= -A\sin x. \end{equation*}
    Substituting these into (3.3.11) gives
    \begin{equation*} -A\sin x+ 9A\sin x = \sin x, \end{equation*}
    i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} 8A\sin x = \sin x, \end{equation*}
    an identity in \(x.\) This implies \(A = \frac{1}{8}.\) Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} y_p = \frac{1}{8}\sin x \end{equation*}
    is a particular solution.
    🔗
    To determine the complementary function of the general solution, the auxiliary equation for the associated homogeneous equation
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 9y =0 \end{equation*}
    is
    \begin{equation*} \lambda^2 -9 =0, \end{equation*}
    whose roots are \(\lambda_1 = 3\) and \(\lambda_1 = -3.\) Then the complementary function \(y_h\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} y_h = c_1 e^{3x} + c_2 e^{-3x}, \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants. Finally, the general solution of (3.3.11) is
    \begin{equation*} y = y_h(x)+y_p(x) = c_1 e^{3x} + c_2 e^{-3x}+\frac{1}{8}\sin x, \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.3.7.

    1. The method of undetermined coefficients is most effective when the forcing function \(f(x)\) is a polynomial, exponential, sine, cosine, or a linear combination of these functions.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. When variation of parameters may involve complicated integrals, the method of undetermined coefficients may be a quicker alternative technique for finding particular solutions of equations with constant coefficients and simple combination of elementary functions for \(f(x)\) remarked in part (i). The method of undetermined coefficients, however, requires a well-informed guessing for \(y_p\text{,}\) which makes it challenging to apply even when \(f(x)\) is a common function, such as \(\sec x\) or \(\tan x\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. A particular solution of a differential equation is just one member of the family represented by its general solution, and thus infinitely many particular solutions exist. In Example 3.3.6, the guess for \(y_p\) was relatively easy to make by observing that the equation has no first derivative term and its forcing function is \(\sin x.\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In the next example, the equation also has a first derivative term, so the trial solution used for \(y_p\) in Example 3.3.6 does not work.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.8.

    Find a particular solution of
    \begin{equation} y'' -6y'+ 9y = \sin x\tag{3.3.12} \end{equation}
    and then write down the general solution.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, we use the same trial solution as in Example 3.3.6 and see what happens. Let
    \begin{equation*} y_p = A\sin x \end{equation*}
    be a particular solution, where \(A\) is a constant to be determined. Differentiating yields
    \begin{equation*} y_p' = A\cos x, \quad y_p''= -A\sin x. \end{equation*}
    Substituting these into (3.3.12) would give
    \begin{equation*} -A\sin x-6A\cos x+ 9A\sin x = \sin x, \end{equation*}
    i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} (8A-1)\sin x -6A\cos x = 0, \end{equation*}
    which is not an identity in \(x\) for any value of \(A\) because \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\) are linearly independent. Thus, no value of \(A\) makes \(y_p = A\sin x\) a valid particular solution. This occurs because \(\cos x\) is involved in the process. Therefore, we modify our trial function for \(y_p\) and take
    \begin{equation*} y_p = A\sin x + B\cos x \end{equation*}
    where \(A\) and \(B\) are constants to be determined. Differentiating twice gives
    \begin{equation*} y_p' = A\cos x-B\sin x, \quad y_p''= -A\sin x - B\cos x. \end{equation*}
    Substituting these into (3.3.12) gives
    \begin{equation*} (-A\sin x - B\cos x)-6(A\cos x-B\sin x)+9(A\sin x + B\cos x) = \sin x, \end{equation*}
    i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} (8A+6B) \sin x +(8B-6A)\cos x =\sin x, \end{equation*}
    an identity in \(x.\) Since \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\) are linearly independent, we obtain
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} 8A+6B=1,\\ 8B-6A=0. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Solving these equations for \(A\) and \(B\) yields \(A = \frac{2}{25}\) and \(B= \frac{3}{50}.\) Thus,
    \begin{equation*} y_p =\frac{2}{25}\sin x+\frac{3}{50}\cos x \end{equation*}
    is a particular solution.
    🔗
    To determine the complementary function of the general solution of (3.3.12), the auxiliary equation for the associated homogeneous equation
    \begin{equation*} y'' -6y'+ 9y =0 \end{equation*}
    is
    \begin{equation*} \lambda^2 -6\lambda +9 =0, \end{equation*}
    which has a repeated root \(\lambda = 3.\) Then the complementary function \(y_h\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} y_h = c_1 e^{3x} + c_2 xe^{3x}, \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants. Hence the general solution of (3.3.12) is
    \begin{equation*} y = y_h(x)+y_p(x) = c_1 e^{3x} + c_2 xe^{3x}+\frac{2}{25}\sin x+\frac{3}{50}\cos x, \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In the previous two examples, we chose trial solutions \(y_p\) before finding the complementary function without any difficulty. In general, however, a trial solution may include terms that duplicate in the complementary function, which are redundant (even useless) since they satisfy the associated homogeneous equation. Therefore, it is useful to determine the complementary function first and then select a trial function for a particular solution. We will typically follow this procedure from now on.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.9.

    Find the general solution of
    \begin{equation} y'' -4y'+ 4y = e^{2x}.\tag{3.3.13} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, we find the complementary function. The auxiliary equation for the associated homogeneous equation
    \begin{equation*} y'' -4y'+ 4y =0 \end{equation*}
    is
    \begin{equation*} \lambda^2 -4\lambda +4 =0, \end{equation*}
    which has a repeated root \(\lambda = 2.\) Then the complementary function \(y_h\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} y_h = c_1 e^{2x} + c_2 xe^{2x}, \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants.
    🔗
    Next, we choose a trial solution for the particular solution. Since the right-hand side is \(e^{2x}\text{,}\) and this term appears in the complementary function, we need to multiply by \(x^2\text{.}\) Thus, we take
    \begin{equation*} y_p = Ax^2e^{2x} \end{equation*}
    where \(A\) is a constant to be determined. Differentiating twice gives
    \begin{equation*} y_p' = Ae^{2x}(2x^2 + 2x), \quad y_p''= Ae^{2x}(4x^2 + 8x + 4). \end{equation*}
    Substituting these into (3.3.13) gives
    \begin{equation*} Ae^{2x}(4x^{2} + 8x + 4)-4Ae^{2x}(2x^{2} + 2x)+4Ax^{2}e^{2x}= e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    This yields
    \begin{equation*} 4Ae^{2x}= e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, \(A= 1/4\) and
    \begin{equation*} y_{p} = \frac{1}{4}x^{2} e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    Hence the general solution is
    \begin{equation*} y =c_{1} e^{2x}+ c_{2} xe^{2x}+ \frac{1}{4}x^{2} e^{2x}, \end{equation*}
    where \(c_{1}\) and \(c_{2}\) are arbitrary constants.

    Example 3.3.10.

    Find the general solution of
    \begin{equation} y'' +y = \cos x.\tag{3.3.14} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Solution.
    The auxiliary equation of the associated homogeneous equation
    \begin{equation*} y''+y=0 \end{equation*}
    is
    \begin{equation*} \lambda^2 + 1=0, \end{equation*}
    whose complex roots are
    \begin{equation*} \lambda = \pm i. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the complementary function of the general solution of the nonhomogeneous equitation is
    \begin{equation*} y_h = c_1 \cos x+ c_2 \sin x, \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) arbitrary constants. If \(y_h\) was not known in advance, we might initially guess
    \begin{equation*} y_p = A\cos x + B\sin x, \end{equation*}
    with \(A\) and \(B\) as undetermined coefficients. However, this form of \(y_p\) duplicates \(y_h\) and it cannot satisfy the nonhomogeneous equation. A point of action is to modify \(y_p\) to
    \begin{equation*} y_p = x( A\cos x + B\sin x). \end{equation*}
    This will work! Let us proceed to determine \(A\) and \(B.\) Differentiating \(y_p\) gives
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} y_p'&= A\cos x + B\sin x+ x( -A\sin x + B\cos x),\\ y_p''&= -2A\sin x + 2B\cos x+ x( -A\cos x - B\sin x). \end{split} \end{equation*}
    Substituting these into the nonhomogeneous equation (3.3.14) yields
    \begin{equation*} -2A\sin x + 2B\cos x = \cos x. \end{equation*}
    For this to be an identity in \(x,\) we must have
    \begin{equation*} A= 0, \quad B= \frac{1}{2}, \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation*} y_p = \frac{1}{2}x\sin x \end{equation*}
    is a particular solution of (3.3.14), and therefore its general solution is
    \begin{equation*} y = c_1 \cos x + c_2 \sin x +\frac{1}{2}x\sin x, \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) arbitrary constants.

    When the forcing function can be decomposed into a sum of several forcing functions, then the sum of particular solutions with these individual forcing functions yields a particular solution of the original equation. The following theorem addresses this property.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.3.11.

    Let \(a_0(x), a_1(x), \dots, a_{n-1}(x)\) and \(f_i(x), \quad i = 1, \dots, k,\) be continuous on an interval \(I.\) Suppose \(y_{p_i},\; i = 1, \dots, k,\) is a solution of
    \begin{equation} y^{(n)}+ a_{n-1}(x)y^{(n-1)}+ \cdots+ + a_1(x) y' + a_0(x) y = f_i(x),\tag{3.3.15} \end{equation}
    for each \(i = 1, \dots, k.\) Then the function
    \begin{equation*} y_p= \sum_{j=1}^ky_{p_j}(x) \end{equation*}
    is a solution of
    \begin{equation} y^{(n)}+ a_{n-1}(x)y^{(n-1)}+ \cdots + a_1(x) y' + a_0(x) y = \sum_{j=1}^kf_j(x)\tag{3.3.16} \end{equation}
    on \(I\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Define the linear differential operator \(L\) on the class of \(n\)-times differentiable functions by
    \begin{equation*} L[y](x) = y^{(n)}+ a_{n-1}(x)y^{(n-1)}+ \cdots+ + a_1(x) y' + a_0(x) y. \end{equation*}
    Since each \(y_{p_j}\) satisfies (3.3.15), we have
    \begin{equation*} L[y_{p_j}](x)= f_j(x) \quad \mbox{on } I,\quad j = 1, \dots, k. \end{equation*}
    Then the linearity of the differential operator \(L\) implies
    \begin{equation*} L\left[\sum_{j=1}^ky_{p_j}\right](x) =\sum_{j=1}^kL[y_{p_j}](x)= \sum_{j=1}^kf_j(x). \end{equation*}
    This shows that the function
    \begin{equation*} y_p= \sum_{j=1}^ky_{p_j} \end{equation*}
    is a solution of (3.3.15) on \(I.\)

    Example 3.3.12.

    Find first a particular solution and then the general solution of
    \begin{equation} y''+4y = 4\sin(2x)+8 \sin x+ 2x^2 -4x+ 3 e^x.\tag{3.3.17} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Solution.
    The auxiliary equation of the associated homogeneous equation
    \begin{equation*} y''+4y=0 \end{equation*}
    is
    \begin{equation*} \lambda^2 +4=0 \end{equation*}
    whose complex roots are \(\pm 2i.\) Therefore, the complementary function of the general solution of (3.3.17) is
    \begin{equation*} y_h = c_1\cos x + c_2 \sin x, \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants.
    🔗
    To find a particular solution of (3.3.17), we split the forcing term into four simpler terms:
    \begin{align*} &f_1(x)=4\sin(2x), \quad f_2(x)=8\sin x, \\ & f_3(x)=2x^2-4x, \quad f_4(x)=3e^x. \end{align*}
    In view of Theorem 3.3.11, we find particular solutions \(y_{p_1},y_{p_2},y_{p_3},y_{p_4}\) and then add them together.
    🔗
    Since \(\sin(2x)\) in \(f_1(x)= 4\sin(2x)\) is a solution of the homogeneous equation, we take
    \begin{equation*} y_{p_1}(x) = x(A\cos(2x) + B\sin(2x)), \end{equation*}
    and find \(A=-1\text{,}\) \(B=0\text{.}\) Thus,
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{y_{p_1}(x) = -x \cos(2x).} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    For \(f_2(x) =8 \sin x,\) we take
    \begin{equation*} y_{p_2}(x) = C \cos x + D \sin x \end{equation*}
    and find \(C=0\text{,}\) \(D=\frac{8}{3}.\) Thus,
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{y_{p_2}(x) = \frac{8}{3} \sin x.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    For \(f_3(x)= 2x^2-4x\text{,}\) we try the quadratic
    \begin{equation*} y_{p_3}(x) = E x^2 + F x + G. \end{equation*}
    and find \(E=\frac12\text{,}\) \(F=-1\text{,}\) \(G=-\frac14.\) Thus,
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{y_{p_3}(x) = \frac12 x^2 - x - \frac14.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    For \(f_4(x)=3 e^x\text{,}\) we take
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_{p_4}(x) = H e^x. \end{equation*}
    and find \(H = \frac{3}{5}.\) Thus,
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{y_{p_4}(x) = \frac{3}{5} e^x.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    By Theorem 3.3.11, a particular solution of (3.3.17) is given by
    \begin{align*} y_p (x)&= y_{p_1}(x) + y_{p_2}(x) + y_{p_3}(x) + y_{p_4}(x) \\ &= -x \cos(2x) + \frac{8}{3} \sin x + \frac12 x^2 - x - \frac14 + \frac{3}{5} e^x. \end{align*}
    🔗
    Hence the general solution of (3.3.17) is
    \begin{align*} y(x) &= y_h(x) + y_p(x)\\ &= c_1 \cos x + c_2 \sin x -x \cos(2x) + \frac{8}{3} \sin x \\ &\qquad+ \frac12 x^2 - x - \frac14 + \frac{3}{5} e^x, \end{align*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants.

    Subsection 3.3.3 Method of Operator Factorization

    In this subsection, we discuss yet another method for finding particular solutions of a second order nonhomogeneous linear equations of the form
    \begin{equation} y'' + a_1(x) y' + a_0(x) y = f(x),\tag{3.3.18} \end{equation}
    where \(a_1(x), a_0(x), f(x)\) are continuous on some interval \(I\text{.}\) For simplicity, we will restrict our attention to equations with constant coefficients and include only a few examples involving variable coefficients. The method extends naturally to higher-order linear equations, which we will briefly discuss in Section 3.4.
    🔗
    We let \(D\) denote the differential operator \(\frac{d}{dx}\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} Dy = \frac{dy}{dx}. \end{equation*}
    Higher order differential operators are denoted by integer powers of \(D.\) For example,
    \begin{equation*} D^2y = \frac{d^2y}{dx^2},\quad D^3y = \frac{d^3y}{dx^3}, \end{equation*}
    and so forth. The differential operator of order \(0\) is the identity operator denoted by \(D^0\) and defined by
    \begin{equation*} D^0y=y. \end{equation*}
    With these notations, the second order equation (3.3.18) can be written as
    \begin{equation*} D^2y + a_1(x) Dy+a_0(x)y= f(x), \end{equation*}
    or more concisely,
    \begin{equation*} p(D)y= f(x), \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} p(D) = D^2 + a_1(x) D+a_0(x) \end{equation*}
    denotes the corresponding second order differential operator.
    🔗
    To introduce the operator method, we begin by considering the first order linear equation
    \begin{equation} y'+a_1(x) y = f(x),\tag{3.3.19} \end{equation}
    where \(a_1(x)\) and \(f(x)\) are continuous on an interval \(I.\) The equation in operator notation can be written as
    \begin{equation*} \big(D+a_1(x)\big)y = f(x). \end{equation*}
    Formally, a solution may be expressed as
    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{1}{D+a_1(x)}f(x). \end{equation*}
    Since a solution of (3.3.19) is given by
    \begin{equation} \begin{split} y&=\frac{1}{D+a_1(x)}f(x)\\ & = c_1e^{-\int a_1(x)\,dx} +e^{-\int a_1(x)\,dx} \int\left( f(x) \,e^{\int a_1(x)\,dx}\right)\, dx, \end{split}\tag{3.3.20} \end{equation}
    where \(c_1\) is an arbitrary constant. A particular solution \(y_p\) (with \(c_1=0\)) is given by
    \begin{equation} \boxed{y_p= e^{-\int a_1(x)\,dx} \int f(x) \,e^{\int a_1(x)\,dx}\, dx,}\tag{3.3.21} \end{equation}
    In particular, if \(a_1(x) =-k,\) a constant, then the formula (3.3.21) takes the form
    \begin{equation} \boxed{y_p =\frac{1}{D-k}f(x)= e^{kx} \int f(x) e^{-kx}\, dx.}\tag{3.3.22} \end{equation}
    🔗
    We next study (3.3.18) in the case where \(a_0\) and \(a_1\) are constants. When formally substituting \(D\) with \(\lambda\) in
    \begin{equation*} p(D) = D^2 + a_1(x) D+a_0(x), \end{equation*}
    we get
    \begin{equation*} p(\lambda) = \lambda^2 +a_1\lambda +a_0, \end{equation*}
    which is the characteristic polynomial for the homogeneous equation
    \begin{equation*} y''+a_1y+a_0y=0. \end{equation*}
    If the polynomial \(p(\lambda)\) factors as
    \begin{equation*} p(\lambda)= (p-\lambda_1)(p-\lambda_2), \end{equation*}
    where \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\) may be real or complex, then the operator \(p(D)\) factors correspondingly as
    \begin{equation*} p(D) = (D-\lambda_1)(D-\lambda_2). \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(p(D)\) can be written a composition of two first order differential operators. Moreover, we find
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} (D-\lambda_1)(D-\lambda_2)y& = (D-\lambda_1)(Dy-\lambda_2y)\\ &=D(Dy-\lambda_2y)-\lambda_1(Dy-\lambda_2y)\\ &=D^2y- \lambda_2Dy- \lambda_1Dy+ \lambda_1\lambda_2y\\ &=D^2y- (\lambda_1+\lambda_2)Dy+ \lambda_1\lambda_2y.\ \end{split} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\lambda_1+\lambda_2 = \lambda_2+\lambda_1\) and \(\lambda_1\lambda_2= \lambda_2\lambda_1,\) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} p(D) =(D-\lambda_1)(D-\lambda_2)y= (D-\lambda_2)(D-\lambda_1)y, \end{equation*}
    and hence the factors commute. A particular solution of
    \begin{equation*} p(D)y = f(x) \end{equation*}
    is expressed formally by
    \begin{equation*} y_p = \frac{1}{p(D)}f(x) = \frac{1}{D-\lambda_1}\frac{1}{D-\lambda_2} f(x). \end{equation*}
    In this situation, a solution of
    \begin{equation*} p(D)y = f(x) \end{equation*}
    can be written formally as
    \begin{equation} \boxed{y = \frac{1}{p(D)}f(x) =\frac{1}{D-\lambda_1}\; \frac{1}{D-\lambda_2} f(x),}\tag{3.3.23} \end{equation}
    and it can be obtained by applying (3.3.22) successively to these two first order operators.
    🔗
    Thus, the method of operators is based on the following idea: if we can factor the differential operator \(p(D)\) into a product of two first order operators, say \(p(D) = (D - \alpha)(D - \beta),\) then we can solve the equation (3.3.18) by solving two first order equations in succession. We will illustrate this method with several examples. These techniques extend naturally to higher order linear equations.
    🔗
    The following example illustrates these techniques.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.14.

    Find the general solution of
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 5y' + 6y = 4x e^x \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    The equation in the operator form is
    \begin{equation*} p(D)y =4xe^x, \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} p(D)= D^2-5D+6=(D-2)(D-3). \end{equation*}
    By (3.3.23), a particular solution is formally given by
    \begin{equation*} y_p = \frac{1}{D-2}\frac{1}{D-3} 4xe^x. \end{equation*}
    Using (3.3.22) and integrating by parts, we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{D-3} xe^x = e^{3x}\int 4xe^x e^{-3x}\, dx = 4e^{3x}\int x e^{-2x}\, dx = -(2x+1)e^x. \end{equation*}
    Consequently, again by (3.3.22) and integrating by parts, we obtain
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} y_p& =-\frac{1}{D-2}(2x+1)e^x \\ &= -e^{2x}\int (2x+1)e^x e^{-2x}\, dx \\ &=e^{2x}\int (2x+1)e^{-x}\, dx \\ &=(2x+3)e^x. \end{split} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Hence the general solution of the nonhomogeneous equation is
    \begin{equation*} y = c_1 e^{2x} + c_2 e^{3x} + (2x+3)e^x, \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are arbitrary constants.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The formula (3.3.23) can sometimes be more conveniently used using partial fraction decomposition:
    \begin{equation} \boxed{\begin{split}y_p &= \frac{1}{D-\lambda_1}\; \frac{1}{D-\lambda_2} f(x)\\ &= \left(\frac{A_1}{D-\lambda_1}+ \frac{A_2}{D-\lambda_2}\right)f(x),\end{split}}\tag{3.3.24} \end{equation}
    with the constants \(A_1\) and \(A_2\) to be evaluated. We can then evaluate the individual terms by using (3.3.22). Let us revisit Example 3.3.14 with this technique.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.15.

    Find a particular solution \(y_p\) of
    \begin{equation*} y''-5y'+6y= 4xe^{x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    In Example 3.3.14, we have
    \begin{equation*} y_p = \frac{1}{D-2}\frac{1}{D-3} 4xe^x. \end{equation*}
    Suppose \(A_1\) and \(A_2\) are constants for which
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{D-2}\frac{1}{D-3} = \frac{A_1}{D-2}+\frac{A_2}{D-3}. \end{equation*}
    Then we find \(A_1= -1\) and \(A_2= 1.\) In view of (3.3.24), we have
    \begin{equation*} y_p=-\frac{1}{D-2} 4xe^x+ \frac{1}{D-3}4xe^x, \end{equation*}
    which, by (3.3.22) and integration by parts, yields
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} y_p &= -4e^{2x} \int xe^x e^{-2x}\, dx + 4e^{3x} \int xe^x e^{-3x}\, dx\\ &= -4e^{2x} \int x e^{-x}\, dx + 4e^{3x} \int x e^{-2x}\, dx\\ &=e^x(4x+4)+e^x(-2x-1)\\ &=(2x+3)e^x.\end{split} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The partial fraction decomposition method employed in Example 3.3.15 works naturally for higher order equations as well.
    🔗
    The next example shows how we deal with the case when certain factor \(D-\lambda\) is repeated.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.16.

    Find a particular solution \(y_p\) of
    \begin{equation*} y''-4y'+4y= 4e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    The equation in the operator form is
    \begin{equation*} p(D)y =4e^{2x}, \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} p(D)= D^2-4D+4=(D-2)^2. \end{equation*}
    By (3.3.23), a particular solution is formally given by
    \begin{equation*} y_p = \frac{1}{D-2}\frac{1}{D-2} 4e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    By (3.3.22), we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{D-2} 4e^{2x} = e^{2x}\int4e^{2x}e^{-2x}\, dx=4xe^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    Then, by (3.3.22), we obtain
    \begin{equation*} y_p = \frac{1}{D-2}4xe^{2x} =e^{2x}\int4xe^{2x}e^{-2x}\, dx = e^{2x}\int4x\, dx = 2x^2e^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Whenever \(f(x)\) contains a factor of the form \(e^{\alpha x},\) a more convenient method exists. To discuss this, suppose \(f(x) = e^{\alpha x} g(x)\) for some continuous function \(g(x).\) Suppose
    \begin{equation*} p(D) = (D-\lambda_1) (D-\lambda_2), \end{equation*}
    \(\lambda_1\) and \(\lambda_2\) real or complex constants. Then
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} (D-\lambda_2)(e^{\alpha x} g(x)) & = e^{\alpha x} Dg(x)+\alpha e^{\alpha x} g(x)- \lambda_2 e^{\alpha x} g(x)\\ &=e^{\alpha x} (D+\alpha-\lambda_2)g(x). \end{split} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} p(D)(e^{\alpha x} g(x))&=(D-\lambda_1)(D-\lambda_2) (e^{\alpha x} g(x))\\ &=(D-\lambda_1)\big(e^{\alpha x} (D+\alpha-\lambda_2)g(x)\big)\\ &=e^{\alpha x} (D+\alpha-\lambda_1)(D+\alpha-\lambda_2)g(x)\\ &= e^{\alpha x} p(D+\alpha)g(x). \end{split} \end{equation*}
    Thus, we have
    \begin{equation} p(D)(e^{\alpha x} g(x)) =e^{\alpha x} p(D+\alpha)g(x).\tag{3.3.25} \end{equation}
    We also have a similar property of the inverse operator; namely,
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{p(D)} e^{\alpha x} g(x) = e^{\alpha x}\frac{1}{p(D+\alpha)}g(x).\tag{3.3.26} \end{equation}
    To see this, using (3.3.25), we have
    \begin{equation*} p(D)\left(e^{\alpha x}\frac{1}{p(D+\alpha)}g(x)\right) =e^{\alpha x}p(D+\alpha) \frac{1}{p(D+\alpha)}g(x)= e^{\alpha x}g(x), \end{equation*}
    which yields (3.3.26).
    🔗
    The formulas (3.3.25) and (3.3.26) are called the exponential shifting properties of \(p(D)\) and its inverse operator, respectively. The next example provides an application of the exponential shifting properties.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.17.

    Find a particular solution of
    \begin{equation*} y'' - 5y' + 6y = 4x e^x \end{equation*}
    by using the exponential shifting properties.
    🔗
    Solution.
    We can write
    \begin{equation*} 4x e^x = e^x (4x). \end{equation*}
    By the exponential shifting property of the inverse operator, we have
    \begin{equation*} y_p = \frac{1}{D^2 - 5D + 6} (4x e^x) = e^x \frac{1}{(D+1)^2 - 5(D+1) + 6} (4x). \end{equation*}
    Since
    \begin{equation*} (D+1)^2 - 5(D+1) + 6 = D^2 - 3D + 2, \end{equation*}
    we have
    \begin{equation*} y_p = e^x \frac{1}{D^2 - 3D + 2} (4x). \end{equation*}
    By using partial fraction decomposition and then applying (3.3.22) and integration by parts, we get
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} y_p&= e^x \left(\frac{A_1}{D-1}+\frac{A_2}{D-2}\right)4x\\ &= e^x\left(-\frac{4}{D-1}+\frac{4}{D-2}\right)x\\ &=e^x\left(-4e^{x}\int xe^{-x}\, dx +4e^{2x}\int xe^{-2x}\, dx\right)\\ &=(2x+3)e^x.\end{split} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    When the coefficient functions \(a_1(x)\) and \(a_0(x)\) are variable, the factors of \(p(D)\) generally do not commute, as illustrated in Example 3.3.18.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.18.

    Find a particular solution of
    \begin{equation*} y''+(x+1)y'+xy=e^{-x^2/2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    The equation in the operator form is
    \begin{equation} p(D)y =e^{-x^2/2},\tag{3.3.27} \end{equation}
    where
    \begin{equation*} p(D)= D^2+(x+1)D+x. \end{equation*}
    By inspection, \(p(D)\) may be factored as \((D+x)(D+1)\) or \((D+1)(D+x),\) and so which one is it? Direct computation shows
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} (D+x)(D+1)y &= (D+x)(Dy+y)\\ &= D(Dy+y)+x(Dy+y)\\ &= D^2y+Dy+xDy+xy\\ &= (D^2+(x+1)D+x)y\\ &=p(D)y, \end{split} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} (D+1)(D+x)y &= (D+1)(Dy+xy)\\ &= D(Dy+xy)+(Dy+xy)\\ &= D^2y+xDy+y+Dy+xy\\ &= (D^2+(x+1)D+(x+1))y\\ &\ne p(D)y. \end{split} \end{equation*}
    Thus, we only have
    \begin{equation*} p(D)y= (D+x)(D+1)y. \end{equation*}
    Then (3.3.27) turns into
    \begin{equation*} (D+x)(D+1)y= e^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}. \end{equation*}
    Put \((D+1)y =z.\) Then we have
    \begin{equation*} (D+x)z=e^{-x^2/2}, \end{equation*}
    which is a first order linear equation in \(z.\) In view of (3.3.21), a particular solution of this equation is given by
    \begin{equation*} z_p=e^{-x^2/2}\int e^{-x^2/2} e^{x^2/2}\,dx= (x-1) e^{-x^2/2}. \end{equation*}
    The constant of integration is chosen so as to simplify the integral below. Using this \(z_p\) for \(z\) in \((D+1)y =z,\) we obtain
    \begin{equation*} (D+1)y = (x-1) e^{-x^2/2}. \end{equation*}
    By (3.3.21), a particular solution of this equation is given by
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} y_p &= e^{-x} \int (x-1)e^x e^{-x^2/2}\, dx\\ & = e^{-x+\frac{1}{2}} \int (x-1)e^{-\frac{1}{2}(x-1)^2}\, dx\\ &=e^{-x+\frac{1}{2}} \int e^{-t}\,dt\quad \left(\mbox{using } t= \frac{1}{2}(x-1)^2 \mbox{ so that }(x-1)dx =dt\right)\\ &=-e^{-(x-\frac{1}{2})} e^{-\frac{1}{2}(x-1)^2}\\ &=-e^{-\frac{1}{2}x^2}. \end{split} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    🔗

We can construct the general solution by leaving these integrals unevaluated. A sketch of this construction is outlined in Exercises 3.3.4.

    Example 3.3.19.

    Find the general solution of the Cauchy-Euler equation
    \begin{equation*} x^2y''+4xy'+2y=e^x, \quad x>0, \end{equation*}
    using the method of operator factorization.
    🔗
    Solution.
    In the operator notation, the equation reads
    \begin{equation*} (x^2D^2+4xD+2)y = e^x. \end{equation*}
    By inspection, the differential operator can be factored as
    \begin{equation*} (x^2D^2+4xD+2)= (xD+1)(xD+2), \end{equation*}
    and this product is commutative. For the general conditions under which the product of two binomial differential operators with variable coefficients commutes, see Exercises 3.3.4. Then the differential equation reads
    \begin{equation*} (xD+1)(xD+2)y =e^x. \end{equation*}
    Let \(z=(xD+2)y.\) Then the equation in \(z\) reads
    \begin{equation*} (xD+1)z=e^x, \end{equation*}
    which is linear in \(z\text{,}\) that is,
    \begin{equation*} \left(D+\frac{1}{x}\right)z=\frac{e^x}{x}. \end{equation*}
    The solution of this equation is given formally by
    \begin{equation*} z = \frac{1}{D+\frac{1}{x}}\left(\frac{e^x}{x}\right). \end{equation*}
    By (3.3.20), we obtain
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} z&=c_1 e^{-\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx}+e^{-\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx}\int\left(\frac{e^x}{x}e^{\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx}\right)\,dx\\ &=\frac{c_1}{x}+\frac{1}{x}\int e^x\,dx\\ &=\frac{c_1}{x}+\frac{e^x}{x}, \end{split} \end{equation*}
    where \(c_1\) is an arbitrary constant. Since \(z=(xD+2)y,\) we have
    \begin{equation*} (xD+2)y = \frac{c_1}{x}+\frac{e^x}{x}, \end{equation*}
    that is,
    \begin{equation*} \left(D+\frac{2}{x}\right)y = \frac{c_1}{x^2}+\frac{e^x}{x^2}, \end{equation*}
    the solution of which is given formally by
    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{1}{D+\frac{2}{x}}\left(\frac{c_1}{x^2}+\frac{e^x}{x^2}\right). \end{equation*}
    In view of (3.3.20), the general solution is given by
    \begin{equation*} \begin{split} y& = c_2e^{-\int\frac{2}{x}\,dx}+e^{-\int\frac{2}{x}\,dx}\int\left(\left(\frac{c_1}{x^2}+\frac{e^x}{x^2}\right)e^{\int\frac{2}{x}\,dx}\right)\,dx\\ &=\frac{c_2}{x^2}+\frac{1}{x^2}\int (c_1+e^x)\, dx\\ &=\frac{c_2}{x^2}+\frac{1}{x^2}(c_1x+e^x)\\ &=\frac{c_1}{x}+\frac{c_2}{x^2}+\frac{e^x}{x^2}, \end{split} \end{equation*}
    where \(c_2\) is an arbitrary constant.
